## **The Problem**

We are in a health crisis driven by disconnection. The Surgeon General has declared loneliness an epidemic with mortality effects equivalent to smoking 15 cigarettes a day. Youth mental health is in freefall. Governor Newsom signed an executive order specifically addressing the crisis of loneliness among young men and boys. One in four men under 30 report having no close friends. And the acceleration of AI into every corner of daily life is compounding all of it — removing the friction, effort, and human contact that once gave life its texture. The cultural backlash is already here: Jean Case, who helped build the internet, is begging people to log off. Baratunde Thurston is writing poetry asking what it means to be human when machines can do everything. The Guardian is coining terms like "friction-maxxing" because people are so hollowed out by convenience they've forgotten what satisfaction feels like.

Meanwhile, we have the most evidence-based, lowest-cost, most universally effective intervention for all of it — and it's sitting there, publicly funded, largely underutilized by the communities who need it most.

**Nature.**

Time in nature reduces cortisol. It treats anxiety and depression. It improves cardiovascular health. It combats loneliness — not through an app, but through the irreplaceable experience of being in your body, on the ground, with other people, under an open sky. Park prescription programs are spreading across the country because physicians increasingly recognize what the research has shown for decades: nature is medicine.

California has 280 state parks. That's not a recreation system. That's a public health infrastructure network — already built, already funded, already distributed across the state. It is, arguably, the largest untapped preventive health system California has.

And it is systematically failing to reach the people who need it most. Communities of color are three times more likely to live in nature-deprived areas. Non-Hispanic whites make up roughly 63% of the population but between 88 and 95% of public lands visitors. The families carrying the heaviest burden of the loneliness and mental health crisis — BIPOC families, low-income families, families in nature-deprived urban neighborhoods — are the same families who have been historically excluded from the outdoors through segregation, cultural erasure, and decades of systems designed by and for people who already belong.

**But here's the thing most people miss: even when these families want to go, the system makes it nearly impossible.**

California's campground reservation system is a case study in structural exclusion. To get a campsite at a popular state park, you need to know that reservations open exactly six months in advance at 8am Pacific. You need a pre-loaded account on ReserveCalifornia.com — a platform operated by Tyler Technologies, an out-of-state contractor currently facing a class action lawsuit over hidden fees that inflate costs by more than 20%. You need to know which specific campsites fit your family's needs, because the system's taxonomy is so convoluted that what looks like one campground might be listed as a dozen separate entries. You need to be online, ready, at exactly the right moment — because for popular parks, everything books within minutes. If you miss the window, you need to know that cancellation monitoring services exist, that a lottery system is being piloted at a handful of parks, that county and regional parks have separate systems with different timelines, that dispersed camping on BLM and Forest Service land is free but requires knowing where to look.

Experienced campers know all of this. They've built their knowledge over years. They run multi-window setups on booking mornings. They subscribe to paid cancellation alerts. They share tips in online forums. They have the schedule flexibility, digital literacy, and insider knowledge to work the system.

A family that's never camped before has none of it. They look at ReserveCalifornia once, see everything booked, and walk away. The system didn't reject them explicitly. It just wasn't built for them. The result is the same: the public health infrastructure that could help them most remains locked behind a wall of expertise they were never given.

And this is the data infrastructure behind it all: we spent over two years trying to get basic API access from California State Parks to build tools that help these families. Introduced by the CEO of Hipcamp. Passed through four contacts. Months of silence between responses. What we finally received is read-only access through Tyler Technologies' API — which they can modify, suspend, or discontinue at any time without notice. If a mission-aligned nonprofit with a Google.org co-founder and an engineering background can barely get data access after two years, what chance does a family in East Oakland have?

## **The Solution**

**Every family that's been disconnected from the outdoors gets their own AI agent — one that changes the story of who belongs in nature and then handles every barrier between that family and their first night under the stars.**

The agent does what an experienced camping family does instinctively, but that a newcomer family would never know to do — and it does it with the cultural intelligence and personal care that no generic tool can provide.

**It changes the narrative.** Before a family will act, they need to see themselves in the outdoors. The agent delivers personalized, culturally resonant storytelling based on who the family is. A Black father sees Carl's story about feeling safe camping for the first time. A Latina mother hears from families like hers. The agent draws from Outdoorithm Collective's library of real community experiences — not stock photos of white families in North Face jackets, but real stories from real families who look like them, who came from where they came from, who were nervous about the same things, and who came alive.

**It eliminates every friction point.** The agent learns the family's situation — where they live, how many kids, what ages, their budget, their experience level, their transportation options, their schedule — and builds a complete, personalized path to their first trip. It knows every reservation system (ReserveCalifornia, Recreation.gov, county and regional parks), every booking window, every lottery. It monitors cancellations. It enters lotteries on the family's behalf. It finds the parks that match — not just by amenities and availability, but by the experiential intelligence that only Outdoorithm has: which parks feel welcoming for first-timers, which campground hosts are great with diverse families, which sites work for group cooking, what the accessibility is really like versus what the website says. It builds the gear list based on what they already own and connects them to lending libraries. It plans the meals. It maps the drive. It estimates the real total cost — no surprise Tyler Technologies fees. And it connects the family to an Outdoorithm Collective community trip if they want to go with others who are also new, also nervous, also ready to come alive.

**It learns and improves from every trip.** Every family's experience feeds back into the system. Post-trip surveys, community feedback, follow-up engagement data. The agent gets smarter about what works — which park recommendations led to return visits, which trip formats produced the deepest transformation, which messaging resonated with which communities. This is the proprietary data flywheel that no generic AI can replicate, because it only exists through real families having real experiences in real parks.

**It gives government something it's never had.** California State Parks and county health departments get real data on who is actually being reached, what's working, and where the gaps remain. Not static planning tools updated on multi-year cycles — dynamic, real-time intelligence on whether the state's public health infrastructure is serving the communities it should. When counties need to report on prevention outcomes under Prop 1's new accountability framework, when the state needs to measure the impact of its Outdoors for All initiative, when the Governor needs data on whether his loneliness executive order is producing results — this system provides it.
