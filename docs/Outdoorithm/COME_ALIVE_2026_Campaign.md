## The Story We're Telling

We are not leading with "outdoor equity" or "social capital" or "civic infrastructure." Those are the mechanisms. The story is simpler and more primal.

**We've forgotten how to be alive. Nature + community is how we remember.**

Every person in this network knows the feeling of being numbed — scrolling at 11pm, realizing they haven't had a real conversation in weeks, watching their kid disappear into a screen. And every person has had that moment around a fire or on a hike where something woke up.

That's what OC creates. A 48-hour container where families come alive — through rest, nourishment, movement, land, community, and grit. Not a wellness retreat. Not a camping trip. A place where Valencia went from being afraid to sleep without a locked door to her daughter running barefoot through camp with no fear, only joy.

**In the emails:** Lead with the feeling, not the framework. Use "come alive" naturally. Let the stories carry the frame — don't explain it.

**In personal outreach:** Same — describe what's happening on these trips as something extraordinary, then let the math and the invitation do the rest.

**In every communication:**
- **Story first, then math.** The brain needs both — emotion creates the impulse, math gives permission. Neither alone is enough.
- **"If you want in"** — joining, not saving. Donors are participants, not benefactors.
- **Donor-centric language** — "you" and "your," 2:1 ratio over "we/our."
- **Under 200 words per email.** Mobile users dominate. Front-load the message.

## Phase 1: The Quiet Phase

**This is where the campaign is won or lost.** The broader email campaign is the second act, not the main event.

### What to Secure Before Email 1

1. **$20K match confirmed** — If not yet locked, ask 3-5 most likely candidates. "Someone in my network has committed to matching the first $20K in donations dollar-for-dollar" is one of the most powerful lines you can put in an email.

2. **15-25 personal emails/texts to the inner circle** — People who can give $2,500-$10K+ but won't unless personally invited. These are the people with capacity *and* relationship. Target: $15K-$25K committed before launch.

3. **Social proof permission** — Ask early commitments if you can reference them. "Several friends from Google and Harvard have already committed" is powerful even without names.

4. **Total quiet phase position: 50-60% of $120K** — $45K grants and early supporters + $15-25K personal outreach = $60K-$70K committed. This is the number that makes the public launch work.

### The Personal Outreach Message

This is not a pitch. It's a personal invitation from one friend to another. Come Alive framing is in the DNA, not laid on top.

---

**Template (adapt for each person — don't send identical):**

Hey [Name],

Quick thing. Sally and I have 8 trips planned this season through Outdoorithm Collective. Joshua Tree, Pinnacles, Yosemite, Lassen, the works.

Hard to describe what happens on these trips. Families who've never slept in a tent show up and something shifts. Deep rest. Real connection. Kids running free. A dad told us feeling safe in the woods for the first time "changed the narrative." A mom called it the most restorative sleep she'd had in years.

I'm about to send a broader ask to my network, but wanted to reach out to you first. $120K for the full season. 8 trips at $10K each, plus $40K in gear so every family shows up equipped. We've raised $45K from grants and early supporters. A friend is matching the first $20K dollar-for-dollar.

Would love to count you in. Happy to talk if you want to know more.

Justin

---

**Why this works:**
- Opens casual and direct — "Quick thing" is how Justin actually texts
- Stories land fast (empathy circuit) → math (justification) → invitation
- "Would love to count you in" = joining, not helping
- "Happy to talk if you want to know more" = the phone call is earned, not initiated. People who want a deeper conversation will ask.
- No dollar amount in the first touch — amount anchors live in the conversation

**Expected results:** 8-15 responses from 15-25 messages. 5-10 commitments at $2.5K-$5K average. 3-5 conversations that lead to deeper engagement and larger gifts.

**If someone doesn't respond:** Text follow-up 3-5 days later. "Hey — did you see my note about OC? No pressure, just wanted to make sure it landed." Natural and low-pressure.

## After the Gift

Retention is the game. 82% of first-time donors never give again. Speed and identity are the levers.

### Within 24 Hours: Personal Thank-You

The brain's three giving circuits — reward, empathy, identity — cool within hours. A thank-you that arrives while the glow is warm reinforces all three. A thank-you that arrives three days later reinforces none.

**Template (adapt for each donor):**

> [Name] — this means the world. You're the kind of person who shows up for families like Valencia's, and that's exactly what you just did. Your gift is going toward our Pinnacles trip in May — 12 families, three days in the park. I'll send you a photo from the campfire.
>
> — Justin

**Why this works:**
- **Identity-affirming:** "You're the kind of person who shows up" reinforces who they are, not just what they gave. This converts one-time donors to repeat givers at 2-3x the rate of neutral thank-yous.
- **Specific impact:** Their gift → a named trip → a number of families. Not vague.
- **Promise of follow-up:** "I'll send you a photo" creates continuity and anticipation.

**Phone calls for thank-yous:** This is the one place where a call is ideal — donor-initiated or not. A thank-you call with no additional ask triggers the reward circuit through voice and warmth. "Hey, I just wanted to call and say thank you. Your gift is going toward our Pinnacles trip. It means a lot." 60 seconds. No pitch.

### Post-Trip: One Photo, One Line

After Joshua Tree (early April), send a brief update to all campaign donors:

> [Photo of families around the campfire]
>
> 40 people. Joshua Tree. Three days. You helped make this happen.

This is cultivation for the next campaign. Keep it short. Let the image do the work.

## What We Don't Do

- **Manufacture urgency.** Joshua Tree on March 30 is real. We don't need to invent deadlines.
- **Over-polish.** Warm and slightly imperfect beats polished and hollow. If the email feels "crafted," rewrite it.
- **Cold-call anyone.** The email creates the invitation. "Reply and we can talk" is the bridge. Calls happen when donors ask for them.
- **Mix stories with statistics.** One family's story in each email. Not "100+ families served." The empathy circuit fires for individuals, not aggregates.
- **Let donors "own" trips.** "I'll count you in for Yosemite" = joining a team. "I'll save Yosemite for you" = buying a trip. We want the first.
- **Compromise the experience for fundraising.** Trips center the families. Donors are supporters, not guests.
