# Outdoorithm Collective Fundraising Principles

## Our Core Belief

Impact drives income. When people understand what we're building and why it matters, they want to be part of it. Our job isn't to convince anyone. It's to invite the right people into something real.

---

## How We Talk About Money

**We invite people on a journey. We don't ask them to "help."**

There's a difference between "Would you help us?" and "Would you want to be part of this?" The first positions donors as benefactors. The second positions them as participants. We want partners who are joining something, not saving something.

**We present opportunities. We don't beg.**

"Would you want to put your name on one?" is different from "Would you consider a gift of $10,000?" Both involve the same dollar amount. One feels like ownership. The other feels like charity.

**We make asks.**

Endless cultivation without clarity serves no one. If someone understands what we're building and has expressed interest, the right time to invite them in is now. We don't wait for perfect moments. We don't hedge. We ask clearly and warmly.

**We do the math.**

People need to understand what their investment makes possible. For us, it's simple: $10K = one trip = 10-12 families in nature, many for the first time. Clear math builds confidence and removes ambiguity.

---

## We Try to Understand What Giving Feels Like

Giving away money is emotionally complex, even for people with resources. Research shows that wealthy people often feel they "can't afford" to give—not because they're greedy, but because lifestyle creep, loss aversion, and fear of the future are real. A gift that looks easy from the outside can feel like a genuine sacrifice from the inside.

We don't exploit this complexity. But we try to understand it. When someone hesitates, we don't assume they're uncommitted. We stay curious. We make space for them to ask questions or move at their own pace. Pushing harder rarely helps. Being patient and clear usually does.

---

## How We Communicate

**Personal emails, not marketing pitches.**

Our fundraising emails should sound like what they are: personal notes from one person to another. No nonprofit jargon. No "founding supporters" or "propelling our mission forward." No strategic deployment of quotes or callbacks. Just human beings talking about something that matters.

**Stories matter, but they should feel natural.**

A quote from Carl or Valencia can be powerful. But if it's positioned at the top of an email like a marketing hook, it stops feeling like a conversation and starts feeling like a pitch. Stories should emerge naturally, the way they would if you were telling a friend about your work over coffee.

**Reference what people tell you, but don't perform it.**

When someone shares what they care about, remembering that matters. But there's a difference between "You mentioned Big City Mountaineers—I think you'd get what we're building" and a strategic callback designed to show you were listening. The first is relational. The second is technique.

**Keep emails short.**

Respect people's time. Say what you need to say. Stop. For printed letters or longer updates, you have more room—but every paragraph should earn its place.

---

## What We Don't Do

**We don't manufacture urgency.**

We're not going to tell people we "need" their gift by Friday or that we're "falling short" of a goal. But when something is genuinely time-sensitive—we're finalizing the spring roster, or a matching opportunity has a real deadline—we say so plainly. That's not pressure. It's information that helps people act if they want to.

**We don't let donors "own" our programs.**

Language matters. "I'll save Yosemite for you" implies the donor is buying a trip. "I'll count you in for Yosemite" means they're joining a team. We want the second.

**We don't compromise the experience for fundraising.**

Our trips center the experiences of BIPOC families. That's the whole point. We don't casually invite donors to "come see a trip in action" if their presence could change the dynamics for the families we serve. If we ever invite supporters to experience our work, it has to be done thoughtfully, with the community's needs first.

**We don't over-optimize.**

The moment an email starts to feel "crafted"—strategic quote placement, hero framing, pronoun ratios—it stops feeling human. We'd rather be warm and slightly imperfect than polished and hollow.

---

## The Relationship Comes First

Most of the people we'll ask for support are people we already know. Friends, colleagues, people who've expressed genuine interest. With these relationships, we don't need elaborate cultivation strategies. We need honesty and clarity.

For new relationships, we take time. We let people get to know us. We don't make cold asks for major gifts.

But when there's a real relationship and real alignment, we don't wait. We invite people in.

---

## Different Gifts, Different Timelines

For $5K-$25K gifts from people who already know us, we don't wait. When there's alignment and understanding, we invite them in.

For significantly larger gifts from newer relationships, we take more time. A first-time $100K commitment might take 12-18 months of genuine relationship before an ask makes sense. That's not endless cultivation—it's respect for the weight of what we're inviting someone into.

The principle is the same either way: we ask when there's real relationship and real understanding. The timeline varies with the depth of both.

---

## A Simple Test

Before sending any fundraising communication, ask:

1. Does this sound like me talking to a friend?
2. Would I be comfortable if this person showed it to someone else?
3. Am I inviting them into something, or asking them to save something?
4. Is the math clear?
5. Does this respect their time?
6. Have I made it easy to say yes?

If the answer to any of these is no, rewrite it.

---

## What Success Looks Like

Success isn't maximizing dollars from each donor. Success is building a community of people who genuinely care about what we're building and want to be part of it for the long haul.

Some of those people will give $500. Some will give $50,000. What matters is that every one of them feels like a participant in something meaningful, not a target on a list.

We're building a movement. The money follows.
