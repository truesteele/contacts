## 1. How We Size a Campaign

### Build the Gift Range Chart First, Then Set the Goal

Never start with a number and hope the math works. Build a donor pyramid and validate it against your actual prospect pool.

**Steps:**
1. **Lead gift = 15-25% of goal.** If you can't identify 4 credible prospects at this level, the goal is too high.
2. **Top 10 gifts = 50-70% of goal.** Modern campaigns are top-heavy. The Rule of Thirds (Seymour, 1960) — top 10 gifts = 1/3, next 100 = 1/3, everyone else = 1/3 — understates this. Plan for the top 10 to carry half or more.
3. **Work down the pyramid**, roughly halving the amount and doubling the count at each tier.
4. **Apply prospect-to-donor ratios:**
   - Top tier ($5K+): 4 prospects per gift needed
   - Mid tier ($1K-$5K): 3 per gift
   - Base (under $1K): 2 per gift
5. **If any tier fails the ratio test**, lower the goal or restructure. Don't assume prospects will materialize.

### Ground Goals in Actual Giving, Not Capacity

AI ask readiness scores show *theoretical capacity*. Actual giving history shows *real behavior*. In 2025, AI scores suggested $150K+ in capacity from ready_now contacts. Actual Stripe giving: $30K, 75 donors, $100 median gift. The gap is real and is only bridged by direct personal engagement.

**The rule:** Cross-reference capacity data with at least one year of actual giving before setting a goal. First campaigns should err toward achievable — exceeding a realistic goal creates a better story than falling short of an ambitious one.

### The Quiet Phase

**50-70% of the goal should be committed before the public launch.** This is non-negotiable. Donors are far more likely to give to a campaign at 65-80% than one at 20% (endowed progress effect). Campaigns that launch below 40% funded struggle to generate momentum.

For OC, "committed" includes grants, early supporter gifts, and match pledges. Personal outreach to the inner circle is part of the quiet phase — those commitments should be in hand before email 1 goes out.

### Make the Goal Simple

"10 trips. $10K each. $100K total." The math does the selling. If the goal can't be expressed in one sentence tied to concrete outcomes, simplify it. (See principles doc: "We do the math.")

### 2026 Worked Example

$100K total season goal. $35K from grants already committed. $65K in new gifts needed, amplified by $20K match + employer matching:

| Tier | Gift | # Needed | Subtotal | Cumulative | Prospects Needed | Available |
|:--|--:|--:|--:|--:|--:|--:|
| Lead | $10,000 | 1 | $10,000 | $10,000 (15%) | 4 | 27 |
| Major | $5,000 | 4 | $20,000 | $30,000 (46%) | 16 | 34 |
| Mid | $2,500 | 6 | $15,000 | $45,000 (69%) | 18 | 24 |
| Base | $1,000 | 8 | $8,000 | $53,000 (82%) | 24 | 31 |
| Community | $500 | 12 | $6,000 | $59,000 (91%) | 24 | ~50+ |
| Small | $250 | 24 | $6,000 | $65,000 (100%) | 48 | ~200+ |

All tiers pass the prospect-ratio test. Quiet phase: $35K grants + $15-25K personal outreach = 50-60% committed before launch.

## 2. Outreach Strategy

### Channel Roles

| Channel | Open/Response Rate | OC Role |
|:--|:--|:--|
| **Personal email** | 25-29% open | Primary channel for both solicitation and cultivation |
| **Text/SMS** | 98% open, 45% response | Follow-up, urgency, milestone updates |
| **Phone** | 80% response | Thank-yous after gifts, monthly giving conversion, donor-initiated conversations |
| **Direct mail** | 9% response (10x digital) | Not currently used; consider for future major donor cultivation |

**Multi-channel donors give 3x more** than single-channel donors. Integrate channels, but respect each one's role.

### Phone Calls Are Earned, Not Initiated

This is a core OC principle supported by the research. The evidence says:
- Phone is most effective for **thank-yous** (boosts retention 30%), **monthly giving conversion**, and **reactivating lapsed donors**
- Cold outreach achieves only 2.3% success and requires 8 call attempts to reach
- Even warm contacts experience discomfort from unsolicited asks — an unexpected call asking for $5K feels like being put on the spot

**Our approach:** The personal email creates the invitation. "Reply and we can talk" is the bridge. People who want to engage further will signal it. Those donor-initiated conversations are far more productive than unsolicited ones — because the person chose to be there.

This aligns with our principles: *"We invite people on a journey. We don't ask them to 'help.'"* A reply to a personal email that leads to a call feels like joining something. An unsolicited phone ask feels like charity.

### Pre-Campaign Personal Outreach (The Quiet Phase)

Send 15-25 personal emails/texts to the inner circle before the broader campaign launches. This is the most important phase.

**The message:** "Hey — OC is doing 10 trips this season. I'm about to ask my broader network to help fund them. Before I do, I wanted to reach out to you personally. Would love to count you in. If you want to talk about it, I'm around."

Warm, personal, low-pressure. People who want to commit will reply. People who want to discuss will ask for a call. People who aren't ready will see the campaign emails later.

**Expected results:** 8-15 responses from 15-25 messages. 5-10 commitments at $2.5K-$5K average. 3-5 conversations that lead to deeper engagement and often larger gifts.

### Campaign Channel Sequence

| Timing | Channel | Audience |
|:--|:--|:--|
| Pre-campaign | Personal email/text | 15-25 inner circle |
| Day 1 | Email 1: The invitation | Full list |
| Days 2-5 | Text follow-up | Openers who didn't act |
| Day 7-10 | Email 2: Momentum update | Non-donors only |
| Day 10-14 | Text: milestone or match update | Non-donors only |
| Day 14-21 | Email 3: Final push | Non-donors only |
| Post-gift | Thank-you call or personal email | All donors |

## 3. Campaign Structure

### The 3-Email Sequence

**Email 1: The Invitation** (~175 words). One story (empathy circuit), then clear math (justification). One link. "If you want in" positioning — joining, not saving. The brain needs both emotion and reason to act; the story creates the impulse, the math gives permission. Include match if secured. Sent to full campaign list.

**Email 2: Momentum Update** (~150 words, 7-10 days later). New story (different person). Progress update with social proof ("X people have stepped up"). Employer matching reminder. Non-donors only.

**Email 3: Final Push** (~75 words, 7 days before close). Very short. Genuine urgency — the trip is actually happening. Non-donors only.

Keep emails under 200 words (42 seconds to speak aloud). Mobile users dominate opens at 62%. Front-load the message. Use donor-centric language — "you" and "your," aim for 2:1 you-to-we ratio.

### Anchor Amounts

**In email copy:** $1,000 / $2,500 / $5,000. Always ascending order — high-to-low decreases donations by 25%.

**In personal outreach:** $2,500 / $5,000 / $10,000. Let the conversation find the right level.

**Pair amounts with impact:** "$1,000 sends two families to the campfire." Specificity beats vagueness. "Gear for one participant" beats "helps multiple participants" (identifiable victim effect).

### Donation Tiers (Internal Reference)

| Amount | Impact | Channel |
|:--|:--|:--|
| $250 | Sends one family camping | Email (base) |
| $500 | Equips a family with full outdoor gear | Email (mid) |
| $1,000 | Sends two families to the campfire | Email anchor |
| $2,500 | Funds a quarter of a trip | Email / personal outreach |
| $5,000 | Sponsors half a trip | Personal outreach |
| $10,000 | Sponsors a full trip | Personal outreach |

### Post-Gift Stewardship

Retention is everything. Only 18.1% of first-time donors give again. A second gift bumps retention to 38.1%. A third gift: 61.2%. Acquisition costs 5-10x more than retention.

1. **Automated receipt** — Stripe (already in place)
2. **Personal thank-you within 24 hours** — Speed matters: the brain's three giving circuits (reward, empathy, identity) cool within hours, not days. A thank-you that arrives while the glow is still warm reinforces all three. Use identity-affirming language: "You're the kind of person who shows up for families like Carl's" hits harder than a generic "thanks for your generous gift." This is the one place where a phone call is ideal — a thank-you call (no additional ask) triggers the reward circuit directly through voice and warmth.
3. **Post-trip update** — One photo, one sentence. Cultivation for next year.
4. **Second gift opportunity within 90 days** — Monthly giving conversion, specific project need, or year-end campaign. This 90-day window triples retention rate.

### Monthly Giving (Future Priority)

Recurring donors provide 5.4x the lifetime value of one-time donors and retain at 83% vs. 45%. Monthly giving is 31% of online revenue and growing.

**When we build this:**
- Name the program (e.g., "Base Camp Circle") — identity drives belonging
- Frame as daily cost: "$2/day" not "$60/month"
- Suggest monthly amounts at 1/3 of one-time equivalents
- Convert one-time donors in the 30-90 day window (29% who go monthly do so in first 90 days)
- Target "streak donors" — those giving the same amount 3+ consecutive times

## 4. Donor Psychology Quick Reference

These are the effects to keep in mind when designing any campaign. Full research is in the best practices PDF.

| Principle | What It Means | How We Apply It |
|:--|:--|:--|
| **Endowed progress** | People work harder when they see progress already made | Never launch at 0%. Show grants + early commitments |
| **Goal gradient** | People accelerate near the finish line | Progress updates in emails 2 and 3 |
| **Identifiable victim** | One person's story > statistics | Lead with Carl or one family, not "100+ families" |
| **Social proof** | People follow peers | "30 friends have backed the season" / Harvard-Google affiliations |
| **Matching gifts** | +51% average donation, +22% participation | "Your $2,500 becomes $5,000" |
| **Anchoring** | First number calibrates perception | Ascending order. Pre-select "Most Popular" on donation page |
| **Donor-centric language** | "You/your" > "we/our" | 2:1 ratio. 45% retention vs. 27% |
| **Warm > cold** | 14.6% vs. 1.7% conversion | Prioritize people who know us. Cultivate cold for 12-18 months before asking |
| **Identity circuit** | People give to confirm who they are, not just to help others | "You're the kind of person who shows up for families" — not just "thanks for your gift" |
| **Warm glow / joy** | The brain's reward circuit (dopamine) fires when giving feels good | Frame giving as joining something exciting, not filling a gap. Celebrate donors. |
| **Decision friction** | Every delay between impulse and action is a "generosity tax" | One-click donate. Minimal form fields. No extra pages between intent and gift. |
| **Emotion + justification** | The brain needs BOTH a story (empathy circuit) and a reason (prefrontal cortex) | Lead with Carl's story, then back it with clear math. Neither alone is enough. |

**What suppresses giving:** Statistics mixed with individual stories (suppresses empathy circuit). Manufactured urgency. Over-polished copy ("the moment an email starts to feel 'crafted,' it stops feeling human"). Video on donation pages (removing video increased donations 527% in one test). Delayed thank-yous — the brain's three giving circuits (reward, empathy, identity) cool within hours, not days.

## 5. Data & Measurement

### What to Track Every Campaign

- Total raised by source (personal outreach, email, match, employer)
- Donors: count, new vs. returning, by tier
- Conversion rates: emails sent → opened → clicked → donated
- Gift range chart accuracy: did the pyramid hold?
- Pre-campaign commitments as % of goal at launch
- Time to close: when did most gifts arrive?
- Channel attribution: what drove each gift?

### Using AI Ask Readiness Scores

The scores (from `score_ask_readiness.py`) are a **prioritization tool**, not a prediction.

**Use for:** List building (ready_now + top cultivate_first), personalization (`personalization_angle`, `receiver_frame`), channel selection (`recommended_approach`), internal gift range chart modeling.

**Do not use for:** Predicting actual gift amounts, setting public goals without cross-referencing real giving data, assuming capacity equals behavior.

### Cultivation Timelines by Gift Size

| Gift Size | Timeline Before Ask | Touchpoints Needed |
|:--|:--|:--|
| Under $500 | Days to weeks | 1-2 (campaign email is sufficient) |
| $500-$5,000 | Weeks to months | 2-4 (personal email + follow-up) |
| $5,000+ | 12-18 months for new relationships | 4-6 meetings/conversations |
| $5,000+ from existing friends | Can ask now | Personal outreach (relationship is already built) |

### Donation Page Optimization

Every extra click, field, or page between the impulse to give and the completed gift is a "generosity tax." The brain's reward circuit fires on the decision to give — friction after that point works against you. From A/B testing across thousands of nonprofits:

- Remove all navigation and external links (one action: give)
- No video on the donation page (use text + one photo)
- Suggested amounts in ascending order with impact statements
- Pre-select "Most Popular" option (+94% revenue in testing)
- Minimize form fields (phone optional — 37% abandon when required)
- Single-page form with visual progress steps
- Mobile-first (52% of traffic, 126% increase from responsive design)
- Trust signals: SSL, payment logos, 501(c)(3) status

## 7. Lessons Learned

### From 2026 Campaign Planning

- **Capacity ≠ giving.** Always cross-reference AI scores with actual giving data.
- **Build the gift range chart first.** It's the single best tool for validating a goal.
- **The quiet phase is the campaign.** 50-70% committed before launch, or don't launch.
- **Phone calls are earned.** Email invites. Donors choose to call. Those conversations are better.
- **Simple math sells.** If you can't express the goal in one sentence, simplify.
- **First campaigns: aim to exceed.** The story of exceeding $100K is worth more than falling short of $150K.

### From Come Alive 2026 Email Copy Audit (March 2026)

Audited all 26 List A personal outreach emails against the evidence-based best practices PDF and wealthy donor psychology research. Found four systematic issues:

**1. Story-to-numbers suppression (highest impact)**

The Alabama mom story was powerful, but most emails went directly from the emotional moment to budget numbers ($120K, $10K/trip, $40K gear). Carnegie Mellon's identifiable victim research is clear: adding statistics to an individual's story *suppresses* the emotional response. The brain switches from empathy mode to analytical mode, reducing giving impulse.

*Fix applied:* Added a transition sentence ("Here's what it takes." / "Here's what the season looks like.") and paragraph break between story and numbers in all affected emails.

*Rule for next time:* After writing the emotional story, check: is there a paragraph break and a bridge sentence before any dollar amounts? If the story ends and the next thing the reader sees is "$120K," you're suppressing the empathy you just built.

**5. Too many numbers in one paragraph**

The budget paragraph had 7 numbers crammed together ($120K, 8, $10K, $40K, $45K, $20K, $75K). Every number forces the reader into calculation mode — the opposite of what you want after an emotional story. The reader is doing arithmetic instead of feeling.

*Fix applied:* Condensed to two essential numbers + the match: "We've raised $45K toward $120K for the full season, and a friend is matching the first $20K in new donations dollar-for-dollar." The reader instantly grasps: partway there, my gift doubles, there's a gap I can fill.

*Rule for next time:* The reader needs three things from the numbers paragraph: (1) progress toward the goal (endowed progress effect), (2) the match, and (3) what's left. They do NOT need the budget breakdown (trips x cost + gear). That's organizational detail that triggers analytical processing and suppresses giving impulse. Max 2-3 dollar figures per paragraph.

**2. Named identifiable victim**

25 of 26 emails said "a mom from Alabama" instead of using her name. The identifiable victim effect is one of fundraising's most robust findings — a named individual with specific details is significantly more powerful than an anonymous one.

*Fix applied:* Changed to "A mom named Valencia" across all emails.

*Rule for next time:* Get permission to use real names and specific sensory details (what they saw, felt, said) before the campaign starts. "Valencia from Alabama who'd never slept without a locked door" is 3x more powerful than "a family who'd never been camping."

**3. Donor as hero, not organization as hero**

Most emails centered on "Sally and I" doing things. The donor's role was passive — "Would love to count you in." Research shows donor-centric language drives 45% retention vs. 27%. The you-to-we ratio was inverted (~3:1 wrong way).

*Fix applied:* Added "You'd be putting the next family at that campfire." before the closing ask.

*Rule for next time:* Count "you/your" vs. "I/we/our" before sending. Aim for 2:1 you-to-we. Every email needs at least one sentence where the DONOR is the agent of change.

**4. Social proof specificity**

"Early supporters" is vague. Specific numbers create normative pressure — "Join 30 supporters" is stronger than "others have given."

*Fix applied:* Changed "grants and early supporters" to "grants and a small circle of founding supporters."

*Rule for next time:* Use a specific donor count. Update it as the campaign progresses. "Join 49 people who've already invested in this" beats "early supporters."

### Pre-Send Email Quality Checklist

Run through this for every campaign email before sending:

- [ ] **One named person** with sensory details (not "families" or "communities")
- [ ] **Story and numbers separated** by a transition sentence + paragraph break
- [ ] **Max 2-3 dollar figures per paragraph** — budget breakdowns trigger calculation mode
- [ ] **You/your count >= 2x I/we/our count**
- [ ] **At least one sentence positions donor as the agent of change**
- [ ] **Social proof uses a specific number** ("Join 30 supporters")
- [ ] **Matching gift mentioned** with clear math ("your $X becomes $2X")
- [ ] **Under 200 words** for mass emails (personal outreach slightly longer OK)
- [ ] **Amounts in ascending order** if included (low to high)
- [ ] **One clear call to action** (link or "just reply")
- [ ] **No manufactured urgency** — only genuine deadlines (trip date, match expiry)

### Wealthy Donor Psychology — Key Patterns to Address

From the "Why Wealthy People Often Struggle to Give Money Away" research. These are the invisible barriers working against every ask:

| Barrier | What's Happening | How We Counter It |
|:--|:--|:--|
| **Loss aversion** | Giving feels like losing money, even when they can afford it | Frame as investment/joining, not sacrifice. "You'd be part of building this." |
| **Lifestyle creep** | Higher income = higher expenses = "I can't afford it" | Never imply they should stretch. Present the opportunity, let them choose the level. |
| **Scarcity mindset** | Even millionaires fear not having "enough" | Don't ask for a specific amount in personal outreach. Let the conversation find the level. |
| **Compassion deficit** | Wealth reduces empathy (UC Berkeley research) | Identifiable victim stories bypass this. Sensory details activate empathy circuits that statistics don't. |
| **Identity/status fear** | Giving away money feels like losing status/security | Position giving as joining an exclusive group. Advisory roles. "Founding supporter." |
| **Intention-action gap** | "I'll give later" becomes never | Genuine urgency (real trip dates). Low-friction donate link. "Just reply." |

**The meta-insight:** Wealthy donors are often not stingy — they're *scared*. Every element of the ask should quietly address fear of loss while presenting giving as something that makes them MORE of who they already are, not less.
