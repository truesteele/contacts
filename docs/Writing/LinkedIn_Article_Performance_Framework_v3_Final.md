# **Executive Summary: 10 Things to Know Before Your Next Article**

## **The Big Picture**

Your articles operate as a four-stage funnel: Distribution (how widely the hook travels) → Click-Through (who actually reads) → Authority (who bookmarks, follows, comments) → Conversion (who takes action beyond the platform). These are different games with different levers. Optimizing for one often trades off against another.

You're now in **Era 2** (newsletter era), where median impressions are ~12,000—roughly one-fifth of your Era 1 breakouts (78,960 and 43,780). That's not decline; it's the natural fade of your "permission to speak" moment after leaving Google. Future breakouts require manufactured urgency: news hooks or uncomfortable truths.

---

## **10 Crisp Takeaways**

### **On Distribution (Getting Seen)**

**1. Stack authority anchors—institutions aren't required.**
Your top performers don't need "Google" in the title. They stack 2–3 authority anchors: institutional (Google, OpenAI), cultural (Baldwin, Bryan Stevenson), market-demand ("#1 question in my DMs"), quantified ("$700M directed"), or timeliness (48-hour news response). Two anchors minimum for breakout potential.

**2. Corporate philanthropy critique is your reach engine.**
Average impressions by topic: Philanthropy Critique (39,000) vs. AI & Social Sector (7,200). When you want maximum distribution, lead with institutional critique, not Kindora updates.

**3. Prophet-Philosopher voice travels furthest—but converts worst.**
Your Prophet articles average 51,000 impressions but only 6.3% CTR. Viral hooks often satisfy readers without a click. Accept this tradeoff or blend in Teacher elements to create curiosity gaps.

### **On Click-Through (Getting Read)**

**4. Promise a numbered deliverable.**
Your highest-CTR articles (Breaking Into CSR at 13.1%, Undivided at 12.5%) promise specific frameworks: "5 pathways," "three practices." Readers click when they can't get the deliverable from the hook alone.

**5. High reach and high CTR rarely coexist.**
Your 78,960-impression article had 7.1% CTR. Your 13.1% CTR article had 43,780 impressions. The hook that travels widest often gives away enough to satisfy casual scrollers. Decide which game you're playing.

**6. Teacher-Guide voice drives the best click-through.**
Average CTR by voice: Teacher (10.9%) > Builder (9.8%) > Prophet (6.3%). If you want readers, not just impressions, lead with a framework promise.

### **On Authority (Getting Remembered)**

**7. Saves-per-view reveals content quality independent of packaging.**
OpenAI Foundation has mediocre saves-per-impression (1.53) but exceptional saves-per-view (27.9). People who read it find it highly reference-worthy. This is the "low CTR but high value to readers" pattern—worth pursuing for authority-building.

**8. Career-relevant content drives followers.**
Breaking Into CSR generates 2.88 new followers per 1,000 impressions—10x the rate of Undivided. If you want audience growth, write content that advances someone's career.

**9. Faith-forward framing is a feature, not a bug—if you use it intentionally.**
Undivided had low public comments (10) but high CTR (12.5%) and high open rate (62%). Faith narrows reach but deepens subscriber relationship. Use it when intimacy matters more than breadth.

### **On Conversion (Getting Results)**

**10. Platform metrics are proxies—track what actually matters.**
Impressions and saves are means, not ends. The question that matters: did this article generate DMs, speaking invites, partnership conversations, or Kindora leads? Track these manually. An article with 10,000 impressions and 3 qualified opportunities beats one with 50,000 impressions and zero.

Use this workflow before publishing any LinkedIn article.

**1. Define the primary reader persona:** Founder/CEO, CSR practitioner, program officer, nonprofit ED, AI builder, or other. Write to a person, not a crowd.

**2. Decide the job:** Distribution / Click-Through / Authority / Conversion

**3. Pick a voice:** Prophet-Philosopher / Teacher-Guide / Builder-Insider / Bridger-Practitioner

**4. Pick a topic pillar:** Corporate Philanthropy Critique / Career Pathways / Leadership Philosophy / AI & Social Sector / Values Integration

**5. Score four dimensions separately:**
   - Scorecard A: Distribution (0–10)
   - Scorecard B: Click-Through (0–10)
   - Scorecard C: Authority & Relationship (0–10)
   - Scorecard D: Conversion & Opportunity (0–10)

**6. Apply adjusters** — timeliness, authority anchors, controversy level

**7. Check for patterns that narrow audience** (niche tech builds, faith-forward without broad hook)

**8. Plan the derivatives** — which carousel, micro-post, or infographic will you create from this article?

**9. Craft the hook post** — this is as important as the article itself

## **Hook Post Templates**

The LinkedIn post promoting your article is as important as the article itself. Your highest-performing articles shared specific hook patterns.

### **Prophet-Philosopher Hook (For Maximum Distribution)**

The opening line anchors to something recognizable—an institution, a cultural figure, or a provocative claim—then names an uncomfortable truth without resolving it, and closes with an implicit promise that the article will go deeper.

*Example (5 Hidden Tensions): "Baldwin's words have been a constant companion... I'm sharing hard-earned truths about navigating the tensions between profit and purpose."*

### **Teacher-Guide Hook (For Career/Authority)**

The opening surfaces the question you always get asked, establishes your standing to answer it, promises what the reader will learn, and frames the content as unvarnished insider knowledge.

*Example (Breaking Into CSR): "This is the #1 question in my DMs... No sugar coating—just practical insights from someone who's been in the seat."*

### **Builder-Insider Hook (For Engagement/Comments)**

The opening drops you into a vivid scene, names the competing demands you faced, uses sensory or metaphorical language, and closes with a question that invites readers to share their own experience.

*Example (Philanthropy Is Easy): "It's cooking impossible meals while every table orders a different dish: CEO wants a safe PR win. Oakland families need support."*

### **Bridger-Practitioner Hook (For Conversion)**

The opening establishes your position between two contrasting worlds, names the gap, asks the question neither side is answering well, and closes with a clear invitation.

*Example (Nightclub & Library): "I spend my days coding AI tools until 2am. Then I step into nonprofit spaces and it's like walking out of a rave into a library."*

# **Four Scorecards: Distribution, Click-Through, Authority, Conversion**

Rather than trying to predict everything with one score, this framework treats the article as a funnel with four distinct levers. Each scorecard predicts a different outcome and uses different inputs.

## **Scorecard A: Distribution (Hook Post Reach)**

**Predicts:** Post impressions—how widely your hook travels in the feed

**Inputs:**
- Authority Anchor strength (see below)
- Timeliness (response to breaking news)
- Tension/controversy level
- Network activation (tags, gratitude lists)
- Format choices

## **Scorecard B: Click-Through (Views / Impressions)**

**Predicts:** View conversion rate—what percentage of people who see the hook actually read the article

**Inputs:**
- Promise clarity ("5 pathways," "risk ladder")
- Unfinished tension (curiosity gap)
- Specificity of who it's for (reader persona)
- "Deliverable" framing (framework, playbook, guide)

## **Scorecard C: Authority & Relationship (Saves, Follows, Opens)**

**Predicts:** Depth of trust—saves, follower gains, email open rate, reshares

**Inputs:**
- Practical usefulness (can readers apply this?)
- Framework quality (is it referenceable?)
- Identity resonance (does it speak to who they want to be?)
- Values alignment (does it strengthen subscriber relationship?)
- Clarity of stance (do you stand for something?)

## **Scorecard D: Conversion & Opportunity (Business Value)**

**Predicts:** Real-world outcomes—DMs, meeting requests, speaking invites, partnership conversations, Kindora leads

**Inputs:**
- Decision-maker relevance (does this matter to people who can take action?)
- Clear expertise signal (does this position you as the person to call?)
- Implicit or explicit CTA (is there a next step for interested readers?)
- Specificity to reader problems (does this address something they're actively solving?)

This four-scorecard approach prevents optimizing for reach when you actually want opportunities, or optimizing for saves when you actually want DMs.

# **What Drives Distribution (Scorecard A)**

## **The Authority Anchor Concept**

Your framework originally identified "big-name anchor in title AND hook" as the key driver. But your actual top performers violate that rule. "5 Hidden Tensions" (78,960 impressions) leads with Baldwin and "half a billion"—no institution in the title. "Breaking Into CSR" (43,780) leads with "the #1 question in my DMs"—no institution in the title.

What you actually have is an **Authority Anchor**, which can be established through multiple mechanisms:

| Anchor Type | Example | Effect |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |

| **Quantified Experience** | "half a billion dollars," "decade directing" | Establishes your standing to speak |
| **Timeliness** | Responding within 48 hours of major news | Borrows urgency from the moment |

Your highest-reach articles typically combine 2–3 of these anchors.

# **Execution Checklist: What to Do With Every Article**

This section distills best practices into a repeatable checklist. Items marked **[Your Data]** are validated in your own dataset. Items marked **[Platform Best-Practice]** are external heuristics that should be validated over 6 issues.

## **Before You Write**

**1. Confirm the article justifies long-form treatment.** *[Platform Best-Practice: 1,500–2,000 words perform best on LinkedIn—validate for your audience.]* If your idea can be fully expressed in a few paragraphs, it's a post, not an article. Reserve articles for substantive depth that rewards the reader's time investment.

**2. Define your primary reader persona.** Who specifically are you writing for: Founder/CEO, CSR practitioner, program officer, nonprofit ED, AI builder? Write to a person, not a crowd. This improves open rate, CTR, and comment density.

**3. Draft 5–10 headline variations before settling.** *[Platform Best-Practice: 40–49 characters is LinkedIn's sweet spot—validate for your audience.]* Your title is read 8× more than the body, and it becomes the email subject line for newsletter subscribers. Formats that reliably perform: "How to..." and numbered lists ("5 Pathways...").

**4. Stack 2+ authority anchors.** *[Your Data: Articles with 2+ anchors consistently outperform. Reformer's Dilemma and Yosemite—lowest performers—lacked strong anchors.]* Choose from: Institutional, Cultural, Market-Demand, Quantified Experience, Timeliness.

**5. Decide on your voice before drafting.** *[Your Data: Prophet = max distribution but low CTR; Teacher = high CTR and saves; Builder = high comments.]* Use Scorecards A–D to ensure alignment with your goal.

## **While You Write**

**6. Format for mobile readers and dwell time.** Short paragraphs (1–3 sentences) and ample white space keep readers scrolling. LinkedIn's algorithm heavily rewards dwell time. Large blocks of text cause mobile readers to bounce.

**7. Use subheadings to telegraph structure.** Subheadings let skimmers see your outline at a glance. They also break up visual monotony and encourage continued scrolling.

**8. Embed images at regular intervals.** Every article needs a compelling header image (~744×400px minimum). Beyond the header, include an image, chart, or diagram every few scroll-depths. If you reference data or a framework, visualize it.

**9. Avoid embedded video.** *[Platform Best-Practice: LinkedIn data shows articles without embedded video outperform—validate for your audience.]* If you want to use video, publish it as a separate native video post that references the article.

**10. Craft your opening lines for the email preview.** The first 1–2 sentences appear beneath your title in the email notification and feed preview. These lines must hook the reader—pose a question, share a surprising fact, or open with a vivid scene.

**11. Link to 1–2 prior relevant articles.** *[Compounding lever.]* Each new article should reference your back catalog: "If you liked this, here's my deeper dive on X." This increases session depth, views per reader, and positions your newsletter as a coherent body of work.

**12. End with three elements:** *[Your Data: Builder-Insider articles with closing questions get highest comment rates.]*
   - A call-to-action question inviting readers to comment
   - A one-sentence author bio reinforcing your authority
   - A gentle nudge: "If this was useful, subscribe to The Long Arc for more"

## **When You Publish**

**13. Publish mid-week, mid-morning.** *[Platform Best-Practice: Tuesday–Thursday, 8–10 AM local time—validate for your audience.]* Avoid weekends and late nights—engagement drops significantly.

**14. Craft the hook post with the same care as the article.** *[Your Data: Hook post is as important as article content.]* Keep it under 200 words, use authority anchors, create tension that the hook doesn't resolve.

**15. Add 3 relevant hashtags to your hook post.** *[Amplifier, not core driver—effects vary.]* Use a mix: one broad (#Leadership), one specific (#CorporatePhilanthropy), one timely if applicable. Don't exceed 3.

**16. Tag people and organizations mentioned—judiciously.** *[Amplifier, not core driver—only tag those likely to respond.]* If they engage, LinkedIn shows your article to their network. LinkedIn penalizes posts that tag people who ignore them.

**17. Post a first comment immediately after publishing.** Use the first comment to add additional value: a behind-the-scenes thought, a related question, or a link to a relevant resource.

## **After You Publish**

**18. Respond to every comment in the first 60 minutes.** *[Platform Best-Practice: Early engagement triggers wider distribution.]* Your replies double the comment count and encourage others to join the conversation.

**19. Re-engage on days 2–3 to extend visibility.** LinkedIn's algorithm now values relevance over pure recency. Highlight an interesting comment or pose a follow-up question to reignite discussion.

**20. Create 2 derivatives within 7 days.** *[Standard practice, not experiment.]*
   - **One carousel OR infographic:** Take key points and turn them into a slide deck (PDF) or single shareable image. Post with "Full analysis in my article [link in comments]."
   - **One micro-post:** Extract a single insight from the article and post as standalone content with "More in my recent article [title]."

**21. Re-share the article at day 10–14 with a fresh angle.** Only a fraction of your followers see any given post. A second share with new framing recirculates the content.

# **Definitions**

## **Platform Metrics**

- **Post Impressions:** Times the hook post was displayed in feeds
- **Article Views:** Actual clicks through to read the article
- **CTR (Click-Through Rate):** Article Views / Post Impressions × 100
- **Email Sends:** Newsletter emails LinkedIn sent (algorithmic, not subscriber count)
- **Email Open Rate:** % of sent emails opened (confounded by send volume)
- **Estimated Opens:** Email Sends × Open Rate (better comparison metric)
- **Engagement Rate:** (Reactions + Comments + Reposts) / Impressions × 100

## **Rate Metrics**

- **Save Rate (per impr):** Saves per 1,000 impressions (packaging efficiency)
- **Save Rate (per view):** Saves per 1,000 views (content quality)
- **Comment Rate (per impr):** Comments per 1,000 impressions (engagement density)
- **Comment Rate (per view):** Comments per 1,000 views (discussion quality)
- **Repost Rate (per impr):** Reposts per 1,000 impressions (amplification efficiency)
- **Repost Rate (per view):** Reposts per 1,000 views (content shareability)
- **Follower Rate (per impr):** Followers gained per 1,000 impressions (growth efficiency)
- **Follower Rate (per view):** Followers gained per 1,000 views (content-driven growth)

## **Dwell-Time Proxies**

- **Combined Quality Score:** (Saves + Comments + Reshares) per 1,000 views
- **Early Velocity:** Views in first 2 hours (indicates hook post effectiveness)
- **Long-Tail Ratio:** Views after Day 7 / Total Views (indicates algorithmic recommendation)

## **Conversion Metrics**

- **Net New Subscribers:** Subscriber count 72h after publish minus count at publish
- **DMs Mentioning Article:** Inbound direct messages that reference the article
- **Opportunities Generated:** Meeting requests, speaking invites, partnership conversations, leads

## **Framework Concepts**

- **Authority Anchor:** Signal that establishes standing (institutional, cultural, market-demand, quantified, timeliness)
- **Fresh Issue:** Article <7 days old, excluded from baselines
- **Primary Reader Persona:** The specific type of person you're writing for
- **Derivative:** Content repurposed from an article (carousel, micro-post, infographic, video)
- **Confidence Level:** High (n≥6), Medium (n=3–5), Low (n=1–2), Very Low (n=1)
