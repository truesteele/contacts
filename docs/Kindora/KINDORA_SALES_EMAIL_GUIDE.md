## Part 2: Justin's Email Voice

### How Justin Actually Writes Emails

Based on his real outreach, here are the defining characteristics:

**Warm and direct.** No throat-clearing. No "I hope this email finds you well." He gets to the point within the first sentence, but the point is always human first, business second.

**Short.** Most of his emails are 3-8 sentences. Even the detailed ones (like plan breakdowns for consultants) use tight paragraphs and bullet formatting. He never writes walls of text.

**Personal before professional.** He leads with shared context: a mutual friend, a recent conversation, a shared experience. The product comes second. The relationship comes first.

**Casual but not sloppy.** He uses "y'all," "folks," contractions, and sentence fragments. He doesn't capitalize unnecessarily or use formal transitions. But his grammar is clean and his meaning is clear.

**Specific.** He uses real numbers ("over 200 nonprofits," "175K+ foundations," "$25/month"), real names, real places. Never vague.

**Generous.** He offers things before asking for things: free accounts, promo codes, overview decks, calendar links, introductions to his network. He leads with value.

**Confident without selling.** He doesn't beg or oversell. He presents Kindora as something worth trying, not something you'd be crazy not to buy. The tone is closer to "I think this could help" than "you need this."

**Signs off simply.** Just "Justin" at the bottom. No "Best regards," no "Looking forward to hearing from you," no "Don't hesitate to reach out." Just the name.

### Voice Rules (Non-Negotiable)

1. **Never use em dashes.** Use commas, periods, or restructure the sentence instead.
2. **Never use "I hope this email finds you well"** or any variant.
3. **Never use "just circling back," "just following up," "just wanted to check in,"** or any variant of "just + passive verb."
4. **Never use "I'd love to" as a filler.** Use it only when the sentiment is genuine and specific.
5. **Never use these AI-tell words/phrases:** "delighted," "thrilled to share," "excited to announce," "game-changing," "cutting-edge," "revolutionary," "innovative solution," "leverage," "synergy," "holistic," "robust," "streamline," "pivotal," "testament to," "underscores the importance," "broader landscape," "at the end of the day," "in today's world," "it's worth noting," "needless to say."
6. **Never stack present participles** ("...enabling, fostering, empowering...").
7. **Never use the "not X but Y" parallel structure** more than once in an email.
8. **Never use filler superlatives** like "truly," "incredibly," "absolutely" unless quoting someone.
9. **Avoid colons before short phrases.** Use a period or comma instead.
10. **Avoid symmetric triads** ("X, Y, and Z" lists of abstract nouns). If you list things, make them concrete and uneven.
11. **Never claim to have done something Justin hasn't done.** Don't say "I checked out your website" or "I looked into your organization" when Justin hasn't. Instead, express genuine appreciation for their work based on what you actually know. If you need more context, do a quick web search first and write one sentence of specific, honest appreciation. The difference between "I checked out Funding Tribe" (dishonest) and "The work you've done coaching hundreds of leaders on fundraising is really meaningful" (genuine observation) is trust. Justin's credibility depends on authenticity.

### What to Use Instead

- "Hey [Name]" or "Hi [Name]" to open (never "Dear")
- Contractions: "we're," "I'm," "it's," "don't," "you'll," "we've"
- "Folks" instead of "stakeholders" or "team members"
- "Happy to" instead of "I would be delighted to"
- Sentence fragments for emphasis. Like this.
- Questions to close, not declarations
- Real numbers always (never "significant savings" when you can say "88% cheaper")
- The word "just" only when it means "simply" or "recently," never as a softener

## Part 4: Buyer Types and What They Care About

### Buyer Type 1: Nonprofit Staff / Executive Directors (Individual Sale)

**Who they are:** Development directors, grant writers, executive directors wearing many hats at organizations with budgets under $2M.

**What they worry about:** Time, money, learning curves, adding another tool that doesn't deliver.

**What hooks them:** Free tier with no risk. Real matches in minutes instead of 40-80 hours of manual research. Cost comparison to what they're spending now (or the consultant they can't afford).

**Tone in emails:** Peer-to-peer. You're both in the trenches.

### Buyer Type 2: Consultants / Agencies (Consultant Plan Sale)

**Who they are:** Fundraising consultants managing grant pipelines for multiple clients. Often independent operators or small firms (2-5 people).

**What they worry about:** Throughput, consistency, client reporting, whether this replaces or complements their existing tools (often Instrumentl).

**What hooks them:** 10 client organizations under one plan, pooled credits, isolated workspaces per client, the ability to transfer ownership when a client is ready. The timing hook: "Your Instrumentl renewal is coming up."

**Tone in emails:** Professional respect. These people know fundraising deeply. Don't explain fundraising to them. Show how Kindora makes their existing expertise faster.

### Buyer Type 3: Nonprofit Networks / Capacity Builders (Enterprise Sale)

**Who they are:** Associations, community foundations, intermediaries, federated nonprofit systems. They support member organizations with tooling, training, and cohort programming.

**What they worry about:** Standardization across members, adoption rates, proving value to their own board/funders, staff capacity to manage rollout.

**What hooks them:** A multi-org pilot with clear success metrics. The ability to provide standardized tooling without managing individual accounts. Volume discounts. Cohort enablement (training, office hours, templates).

**Tone in emails:** Partnership. You're proposing to solve a problem together, not selling them a product.

### Buyer Type 4: Foundations / Funders (Enterprise Sale, Highest ACV)

**Who they are:** Program officers, COOs, VP of Strategy at private foundations. They fund nonprofits and want those nonprofits to build capacity.

**What they worry about:** Grantee autonomy, avoiding the "capacity-building trap" (buying tools that increase dependency rather than independence), data privacy, whether this actually helps or is performative.

**What hooks them:** The privacy guardrails (foundations can't see grantee activity). The diversification framing (helping grantees find funding beyond the purchasing funder). Justin's credibility as someone who was the buyer at Google.org and saw these dynamics firsthand. The nonprofit digital investment research showing technology is chronically underfunded for grantees.

**Tone in emails:** Insider-to-insider. Justin writes to foundations as a former program officer, not as a vendor. He understands their world.

### Buyer Type 5: Warm Introductions / Referral Follow-Ups

**Who they are:** People connected to Justin through mutual contacts, Camelback fellowship, HBS network, Google alumni, or personal relationships.

**What they worry about:** Whether this is a genuine connection or a sales pitch disguised as networking.

**What hooks them:** The personal connection first. Kindora second. Justin always leads with the relationship and offers something before asking for anything.

**Tone in emails:** Friend-to-friend. Casual, generous, brief.

Here's my calendar if it's easier to pick a time:
https://calendly.com/justin-kindora/1-2-1

**Key principles:**

## Part 6: Situational Guidance

### When Someone Mentions a Competitor

If they mention Instrumentl, Candid, GrantStation, or any other tool:

- Never trash the competitor. Acknowledge that they're useful tools
- Focus on what's different, not what's better: pricing model (no annual contracts), AI-powered matching vs. keyword search, the free tier, the depth of Intel Briefs
- If they're switching from Instrumentl: reference timing around their renewal date
- Use specific comparisons: "Instrumentl starts at $179/month with an annual contract. Our Community plan is $25/month, month-to-month"

### When They Say "We Don't Have Budget"

- Lead with the free tier. It's real and permanent, not a trial
- Offer a promo code that unlocks premium features for 30 days
- For networks/foundations: frame it as capacity-building spend, not software spend. "Many foundations are starting to cover this as part of grantee capacity-building"
- Never pressure. "The free tier is always there. Happy to revisit if budget opens up"

### When They Say "We Already Have Something"

- Ask what they're using (genuine curiosity, not interrogation)
- Position Kindora as complementary if possible: "A lot of our users also use [Tool] for [specific thing] and use Kindora for the funder research and Intel Briefs"
- If they're using spreadsheets: "That's actually what most of our users were doing before. The platform essentially automates the research part of that workflow"

### When They Ask About Data Privacy / Security

- Kindora uses public data only: IRS 990 forms, foundation websites, annual reports
- No AI training on customer data
- Row-level security and encrypted storage
- SOC 2 Type II compliance in progress
- For foundation deals: grantees control their own accounts. Foundations see aggregate usage metrics only, never individual grantee activity

### When They Ask About AI / How It Works

- Don't get too technical. Focus on outcomes
- "Our matching AI reasons like an experienced program officer, not a keyword search engine. It looks at a funder's actual giving history, stated priorities, and geographic focus, then evaluates fit against your specific organization and programs"
- For Intel Briefs: "Think of it as the kind of research a $5,000 consultant would produce, covering leadership, giving patterns, strategic approach recommendations, and key contacts. We generate it in 24-48 hours for $8"

## Part 7: AI Writing Avoidance Checklist

Before sending any email, check for these patterns. If any are present, rewrite.

### Structure Red Flags
- [ ] Email opens with a generic greeting or filler sentence
- [ ] Multiple paragraphs of equal length (real emails have uneven paragraphs)
- [ ] Every sentence is the same length (vary between short punches and longer thoughts)
- [ ] The email could be about any product (not specific enough to Kindora)
- [ ] Closing uses a formal signoff ("Best regards," "Warm regards," "Sincerely")

### Word/Phrase Red Flags
- [ ] Any em dash (replace with comma, period, or parenthetical)
- [ ] "Leverage," "utilize," "facilitate," "streamline," "optimize"
- [ ] "I wanted to reach out," "I hope this finds you well," "I'd be remiss"
- [ ] "Excited to share," "thrilled to announce," "pleased to inform"
- [ ] "Game-changing," "cutting-edge," "best-in-class," "world-class"
- [ ] "This is a testament to," "this underscores," "this highlights"
- [ ] "In today's landscape," "in this day and age," "as we navigate"
- [ ] "Don't hesitate to reach out," "please don't hesitate to"
- [ ] Stacked gerunds: "enabling, fostering, empowering"
- [ ] "Key takeaway," "bottom line," "at the end of the day"
- [ ] "Comprehensive," "robust," "holistic," "seamless" (unless technically accurate)
- [ ] "It's worth noting that," "needless to say," "interestingly"

### Tone Red Flags
- [ ] Sounds like a press release instead of an email from a person
- [ ] Uses the passive voice when active would be more natural
- [ ] Over-explains Kindora's mission/values when the recipient didn't ask
- [ ] Includes more than one call-to-action (pick one clear next step)

## Part 10: Operational Workflow (How to Find, Research, and Draft Sales Emails)

This section describes the step-by-step process for using the Google Workspace MCP tools to identify emails that need sales responses, research the contacts, write drafts in Justin's voice, and place them in his Gmail drafts folder with attachments.

### Step 1: Find Unread Emails That Need a Response

Search the justin@kindora.co inbox for unread messages. Use the `search_gmail_messages` tool with queries like:

- `is:unread in:inbox` (all unread inbox messages)
- `is:unread in:inbox (deck OR overview OR introduction OR intro)` (likely sales-relevant)
- `is:unread in:inbox from:[specific sender]` (if looking for a specific thread)

Then fetch the actual email content using `get_gmail_message_content` or `get_gmail_messages_content_batch` to read the full body, headers, and threading info.

### Step 2: Triage for Sales Relevance

Not every unread email needs a sales response. Filter out:
- Automated notifications (Google Alerts, calendar invites, SaaS product updates)
- Internal team messages that are operational, not sales
- Newsletters, marketing emails, and bulk sends
- Emails already responded to in thread

Focus on:
- Warm introductions from mutual contacts (highest priority)
- Inbound interest or questions about Kindora
- Referral follow-ups
- Emails from foundations, networks, consultants, or nonprofit leaders
- Trigger events (someone mentioning funding challenges, capacity building, etc.)

### Step 3: Research Before Writing

For every email that needs a response, research the person and their organization before drafting. This is non-negotiable.

**Why:** Justin's voice is authentic. He doesn't fake familiarity. But he does his homework, and his appreciation for people's work is genuine and specific. The research makes this possible.

**How:**
1. **Web search** the person's name and organization. Look for: what they do, who they serve, how long they've been at it, any specific programs or achievements.
2. **Extract one specific detail** you can use to express genuine appreciation. Not "I love what you're doing" (generic). Instead: "The work you've done coaching hundreds of leaders on fundraising, especially championing minority and women missionaries, is really meaningful" (specific, true, researched).
3. **Never fabricate familiarity.** Don't say "I checked out your website" when Justin hasn't. Don't say "I've been following your work" when he hasn't. Express appreciation for what you've learned, but frame it as appreciation, not prior engagement.

### Step 4: Identify the Email Type and Select a Template

- Warm intro from a mutual contact → Template 1

- Consultant interested in plans → Template 3

### Threading Details

To thread a reply correctly, you need three pieces of information from the original email:

1. **thread_id**: Gmail's internal thread identifier. Found in the message metadata.
2. **in_reply_to**: The `Message-ID` header from the specific message you're replying to. Looks like `<CALxKrFe...@mail.gmail.com>`.
3. **references**: The chain of Message-IDs in the thread. For a simple reply to the first message, this is the same as `in_reply_to`.

Without these, Gmail creates a new thread instead of continuing the existing conversation.
