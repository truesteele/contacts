# **Why Wealthy People Often Struggle to Give Money Away**

Many people plan to **“make money now and do good later,”** but research shows that increasing wealth doesn’t automatically make it easier to give money away. In fact, affluent individuals frequently find it *harder* to part with their money than they expected. Over time, higher income tends to bring higher expenses, shifting perceptions of what is “enough” and triggering psychological barriers to generosity. Below, we explore key research findings and psychological factors – from lifestyle inflation to loss aversion – that explain why even the very well-off often feel they “can’t afford” to give, and highlight who has studied these patterns.

## **Lifestyle Creep and Hedonic Adaptation**

**Lifestyle inflation** (or “lifestyle creep”) is a well-documented phenomenon: as people earn more, their spending *rises* in tandem, and yesterday’s luxuries become today’s necessities . This means that a higher income often doesn’t translate into a feeling of surplus wealth – instead, the extra money gets absorbed by upscale housing, private schooling, nicer cars, and other costly upgrades. Over time, individuals *adapt* to their higher standard of living (a form of *hedonic adaptation*), so it no longer feels extravagant – it feels normal. Consequently, they may still perceive little “excess” to give away.

* **Never Feeling “Rich Enough”:** Research shows that as people’s assets grow, their internal benchmark for what’s *needed* to feel financially secure rises as well . In other words, gaining wealth can be like chasing a moving target. For example, a survey of U.S. millionaires found that **40% of them did not feel wealthy** despite averaging $3.5 million in assets – on average they said they would need *$7.5 million* to feel rich . Many millionaires reported being *careful* with spending and worried about maintaining their lifestyle in retirement . This illustrates how even a high net worth can *feel* insufficient once lifestyle expectations increase.

* **Spending Expands with Income:** Data confirms that higher-income households often save and donate only a similar *percentage* of their income as lower earners, because their expenses grow accordingly. One analysis by the *Chronicle of Philanthropy* found that Americans earning $200,000+ actually **decreased** the share of income they gave to charity in the late 2000s, while those earning under $100,000 slightly *increased* their giving share . In fact, households making under $25k per year gave **4.2%** of their income, whereas those making over $75k gave only **2.7%** on average . The wealthier had far more dollars, but they devoted a smaller fraction to charity – suggesting that much of their extra income went toward personal consumption rather than philanthropy. Studies also show that many millionaires save at rates only on par with everyone else, meaning they aren’t accumulating a big cushion despite higher earnings . Their bigger paychecks get offset by bigger budgets, leaving them feeling not much more prepared to give (or even retire) than before.

* **Social Comparison and New Norms:** Psychological research indicates that as people move into higher income circles, they continually **adjust their reference group**. They start comparing themselves to even wealthier peers – neighbors, coworkers, friends – and thus *relativize* their own wealth. What might seem like an abundance to an outside observer can feel merely average or even inadequate in an affluent social bubble. This “keeping up with the Joneses” effect reinforces lifestyle creep and can erode the sense that one has *more than enough*. As one financial psychology commentator noted, someone with £20 million may *still* feel they need more money, and even at £100 million many feel anxious about having “enough” . The bar for contentment keeps moving upward. Over half of American millionaires surveyed say they are *not* fully confident they can sustain their current lifestyle for the long term, and one third believe *“it would take a miracle”* to retire securely . In short, wealth is relative – when affluence becomes one’s new normal, the psychological sense of abundance often fades, making generous giving feel less feasible than one originally imagined.

## **Fear of Loss, Insecurity, and “Scarcity Mindset”**

Another major reason wealthy individuals hesitate to give is **fear** – specifically, fear of losing their wealth or someday not having enough. Psychologically, giving money away can feel like a **loss**, and humans are wired to dislike losses. In behavioral economics this is known as *loss aversion*: people feel the pain of giving something up more acutely than the pleasure of gaining something equivalent . For someone who has accumulated substantial wealth, the idea of relinquishing a large sum – even for a good cause – may subconsciously trigger outsized anxiety. Research by psychologists Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman first established this bias, and it **applies even when one has plenty** . The wealthy often remain *very loss-averse*, which can make parting with money emotionally difficult (it “hurts” more than it rationally should).

Several studies and experts have noted that **wealth does not automatically erase financial fears**. Paradoxically, affluent people can retain a *scarcity mindset* – a deep-seated belief that resources are limited and one must hold on to what one has . The *AES International* “Paradox of Wealth” report (2023) observes that many rich individuals worry *more* about money as their wealth grows: *“as their wealth increases, so too does the number that is ‘needed’ to feel secure.”* No matter how high the balance climbs, they imagine scenarios where it could all disappear. This is often rooted in upbringing and personality: for example, those who grew up with financial instability may carry lifelong anxiety about being destitute, driving them to hoard wealth as a protective buffer . One private wealth survey found that over one-third of millionaires agreed that *“it will take a miracle to retire securely”*, underscoring how prevalent financial insecurity is even among the rich .

Key psychological factors at play include:

* **Loss of Status and Identity:** For high-net-worth individuals, wealth isn’t just money – it’s tied to their identity, success, and security. Thus, giving away money can feel like chipping away at one’s *safety net* or even one’s status. The AES report notes that for the very wealthy, losing a chunk of wealth isn’t viewed merely as a financial setback; it’s *“a blow to their social standing \[and\] self-esteem,”* and a threat to the lifestyle and recognition that wealth affords . Some fear that if they give too much and their fortune wanes, they’ll be seen as a failure. Public scrutiny can exacerbate this – wealthy donors might imagine, *“If I give away millions and later run into financial trouble, people will judge me or pity me.”* This fear can be **paralyzing**, leading to *indecision and procrastination* about major philanthropy . In short, the rich often fear not only losing money, but also losing the *sense of self-worth and security* that money provides.

* **Loss Aversion and “Pain” of Paying:** Neurological and behavioral studies find that spending money – literally paying – can activate pain centers in the brain for many people. When considering a large donation, a wealthy person might instinctively focus on the *loss* of funds (“That’s $100,000 less in my portfolio”) more than the benefits of the gift. Because losses loom larger than gains, the *psychological cost* of charitable giving can feel high, even if objectively the person can afford it. This contributes to the *“I can’t afford it”* mindset, surprisingly common among the rich. They might say, *“We’re well-off, but we have a lot of expenses and you never know what can happen – we just can’t give much more right now.”* Their caution is partly driven by an *emotional* risk-aversion – the same force that makes investors over-value safe assets – rather than by an objective inability to give.

* **Maintaining Control in Uncertain Times:** Wealthy individuals also tend to be future-oriented planners (more so than those with less means) . Being future-focused can mean *worrying about worst-case scenarios*. Especially after seeing economic downturns or knowing families that lost fortunes, many rich donors fear a scenario where *“if I give away too much, I might not have enough if things go south.”* In their minds, money equals control over future adversity. Psychologist Paul Piff’s research suggests that higher-class individuals are often **less impulsive** about generosity – they think ahead about their finances, whereas lower-income individuals may act on compassionate impulse in the moment . The wealthy are constantly projecting into the future (“What if I live to 100? What if the market crashes? What if our medical expenses skyrocket?”), which can lead them to hold onto wealth *“just in case.”* As a result, **charitable giving is postponed** indefinitely while they shore up an ever-bigger safety net for imagined future needs.

## **Wealth and Reduced Empathy (“Compassion Deficit”)**

Beyond fears and expenses, research in social psychology has uncovered a striking trend: **as people climb the socioeconomic ladder, their empathy and charitable tendencies often decline** . In other words, gaining wealth can change one’s mindset about others, sometimes making a person *less* generous than they were when they had less. Several studies by psychologists like **Dacher Keltner, Paul Piff, and colleagues** have demonstrated this empathy gap between social classes:

* **Greater Wealth, Lower Compassion:** In experiments at UC Berkeley, upper-class participants (measured by income, education, and self-perceived status) were **less likely to offer help** or show empathy compared to lower-class participants . For example, individuals who rated themselves in a higher social class gave **significantly less** of their winnings to a stranger in need and were less inclined to agree that one should donate a high percentage of income to charity . In one paper, lower-class people said one *should* give \~5% of income to charity, while the higher-class thought \~3% was enough . Real-world data back this up: Americans with lower incomes donate *a higher fraction* of their income than wealthier Americans, on average . Researchers coined the term **“compassion deficit”** to describe why the rich give relatively less – having experienced fewer hardships, they may be less attuned to others’ suffering .

* **Psychological Explanations:** Why would having more resources lead to *less* generosity? One theory is that **wealth increases one’s sense of independence** and reduces reliance on community, which in turn dampens empathy. As *Scientific American* reported, *“as people climb the social ladder, their compassionate feelings towards others decline.”* Wealth can create a bubble of self-sufficiency – if you can pay for all your needs, you may feel others should too. Piff and colleagues found that **lower-income individuals notice other people’s needs more** (“I often notice people who need help” was a statement they agreed with readily) and even show a physiological *attunement* to others’ suffering (e.g. lower-class participants had heart-rate slowdowns – a sign of compassion – when watching a video of sick children) . By contrast, upper-class individuals were **more likely to be distracted or disengaged** during others’ distress and had a harder time reading others’ emotions . In practical terms, someone living paycheck-to-paycheck knows what it feels like to need help, so they’re quicker to empathize and give when others are in need. A wealthy person, insulated by comfort, might not *feel* the same urgency or may subconsciously stereotype the needy as responsible for their own plight.

* **Entitlement and Attitudes Toward Greed:** Studies also indicate that wealthier people are more prone to endorse *entitlement* and pro-self values. In one experiment, upper-class participants were **more likely to agree with statements that justify greed** and self-interest as “good” or morally defensible . This attitude was linked with more selfish behavior – for instance, people primed to feel wealthy took more candy from a jar that was supposed to be for children, and drivers of luxury cars were observed to *yield* to pedestrians less often than drivers of economy cars . Such findings, highlighted by Dr. Paul Piff, suggest that wealth can breed a sense that *“I earned this, I deserve it,”* making individuals less inclined to give some away. Essentially, success can foster an internal narrative that one’s own hard work got them there (and others could do the same), leading to *less* sympathy for those who have less. Indeed, one **University of Toronto** study found that rich participants who believed their wealth was due to their *own effort* were *less generous* than those who attributed it more to luck – because the former were more likely to view the poor as undeserving . By contrast, those who credited their abundance to factors like community or faith tended to feel *more* grateful and gave more freely . Overall, a growing body of psychology research (some of it debated and refined in recent years) supports the observation you’ve made: **as people become wealthier, their habits and mindset often shift in ways that make generous giving harder.**

## **The Intention-Action Gap: “I’ll Give Later”**

It’s also important to recognize a common pattern related to human procrastination and good intentions. Many business school students or young professionals – like the peers you recall – sincerely intend to **focus on philanthropy after achieving financial success**. This mindset is sometimes called *“earning to give”* (especially in effective altruism circles) or simply deferring altruism: *“I’ll make my fortune first, then turn to social impact.”* However, research suggests this approach is a psychological **trap** for many. Once career and family life take off, that “later” keeps getting pushed further out, and the barriers described above grow stronger over time.

Behavioral scientists point out that **delaying difficult actions** (whether it’s dieting, retiring, or donating a large sum) often results in never doing them at all, due to present bias. In the context of charity, if one views *giving generously* as something only appropriate when one is *“wealthy enough,”* that threshold may never be reached. University of Virginia researchers studying generosity note that *“if people view giving as something that only wealthy people can do, they will just wait to be wealthy”* – and waiting tends to continue indefinitely as the definition of “wealthy” changes . The reality is that by the time individuals feel financially comfortable (if ever), they are also accustomed to a certain lifestyle and level of *financial caution*, which makes it psychologically tough to suddenly switch and give away large portions of money.

In fact, some data show that **middle age and pre-retirement years (when earnings peak)** are among the least charitable phases of life for many people. One large analysis of U.S. giving patterns found that people in their 50s and early 60s donated *less frequently* than both younger adults and retirees, likely because this is when they are most focused on securing their own nest egg . After retirement (post-65), some do ramp up charitable giving – perhaps realizing they won’t “lose it all” after all, or for estate planning and legacy reasons – but during prime earning years many hold back. The upshot is that *“later” rarely feels like the right time to give; by then, the habit of not giving is entrenched.* Psychologist Utpal Dholakia observes that lower-income individuals tend to be more *present-oriented* – if they feel a generous impulse, they act on it now – whereas those with higher income are more *future-oriented*, constantly calculating and often postponing charitable acts . The wealthy might tell themselves they’ll donate once they hit a certain net worth or once the economy is more stable, but as we’ve seen, **the goalposts for “enough” keep moving**. This intention-action gap helps explain why so many well-off people who *planned* to be generous later end up giving far less than they could. Good intentions get hijacked by a mix of lifestyle inertia, caution, and the everyday busyness of managing affluent lives.

## **Conclusion: Understanding the Psychology of Reluctant Giving**

In summary, the difficulty you’ve observed – affluent individuals having “an incredibly hard time parting with” their money – is backed up by substantial research in psychology, behavioral economics, and philanthropy studies. Key contributors to this field include social psychologists like **Paul Piff and Dacher Keltner**, who have documented the empathy and generosity gap by social class ; economists and decision scientists like **Daniel Kahneman**, who illuminated loss aversion and how the pain of giving can outweigh the joy of helping ; and philanthropy researchers like **Lalin Anik, Eric Martin**, and others who study what motivates (or fails to motivate) donors . Their findings converge on the idea that **human psychology often counteracts our altruistic intentions as our wealth grows**. Higher income brings *higher perceived needs*, a stronger instinct to protect what one has, and even subtle shifts in values and attention that make it harder to empathize with those less fortunate. Wealthy Americans often truly feel they “can’t afford” to give more, even when outsiders can see that they have plenty – because psychologically, they *don’t experience* it as surplus.

Understanding these dynamics can be very useful, especially for a nonprofit leader like yourself who is trying to encourage generosity. It’s not simply about greed or goodwill; it’s about mindset and fear. For instance, knowing that **fear of future loss** looms large, you might alleviate donors’ anxieties by emphasizing financial planning (showing that a gift won’t jeopardize their family’s security) or by arranging commitments that feel reversible. Knowing that **empathy wanes with wealth**, it becomes crucial to personalize appeals – studies show that wealthy donors respond better when they can *see and emotionally connect* with a specific cause or individual, rather than abstract statistics . Storytelling that vividly illustrates need (the “identifiable victim” effect) can help overcome the compassion gap. Likewise, given that many rich people derive a sense of abundance more from relationships and purpose than from money alone, it can help to **engage them socially**: one UVA study suggests connecting well-off donors with like-minded peers and community efforts, which can increase their feelings of fulfillment and loosen the grip of a solely money-centric mindset .

Above all, recognizing that wealthy individuals are often *terrified of losing what they’ve built* is key. This terror can be mitigated by reframing giving not as a loss, but as a meaningful investment in *something* – whether it’s their values, legacy, or community – that provides non-monetary returns like personal growth and societal impact. Indeed, sociological studies (e.g. **“the paradox of generosity”**) find that people who do give generously often report higher happiness and purpose in life. But to get there, donors must overcome those psychological hurdles. As your experience shows, having “more than they need” isn’t sufficient by itself to spur generosity – it takes conscious effort to break free from the cycle of lifestyle escalation and fear.

In conclusion, the reluctance of well-off individuals to give away money is not just anecdotal – it’s a human tendency analyzed by researchers across disciplines. **Lifestyle creep, loss aversion, scarcity mindset, empathy gaps, and procrastination of good intentions** all contribute to the phenomenon. By understanding who has studied this and what they’ve found, we gain insight into the affluent donor’s psyche: they’re often not maliciously stingy, but rather *human* – swayed by feelings of uncertainty and new normals that make *any* amount of money feel possibly insufficient. This knowledge can inform strategies to encourage philanthropy, but it also reminds us that **wealth alone doesn’t automatically convert to generosity**. Without actively counteracting these psychological factors, “making a lot and then giving it away” tends to be much easier said than done.
