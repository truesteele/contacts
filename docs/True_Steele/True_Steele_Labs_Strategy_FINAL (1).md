# PART I: STRATEGIC CONTEXT

## 1.1 Why This Pivot, Why Now

After one year operating True Steele as a fractional Chief Impact Officer, three realities have emerged:

**The CIO value is real but illegible.** Clients benefit enormously from strategic leadership, but the value is hard to prove. The feedback—even from satisfied clients—reveals a gap between impact delivered and impact perceived. When you're the glue holding things together, nobody sees the glue. One client literally said they were "disappointed we didn't get more done"—despite significant strategic progress.

**Products are undeniably tangible.** When I ship software—the Flourish Fund ATS, the grant innovation portal, Kindora, Outdoorithm—people can see it, use it, immediately recognize value. There's no ambiguity about what was delivered.

**AI has collapsed the cost of building.** What used to require a team of eight engineers and nine months can now be done by one skilled builder in weeks. I've proven this repeatedly. Kindora cost $60k total to build—concept to production. The economics have fundamentally changed, but most of the market hasn't caught up.

Meanwhile, the social sector is caught between urgency and paralysis. Leaders know AI matters. They feel pressure to "do something." But they're overwhelmed by options, terrified of ethical risks, and skeptical after decades of failed technology projects.

They need someone who understands their world AND can actually build.

---

## 1.2 The Honest Context

I'm not pivoting because I'm bored with fractional CIO work. I'm pivoting because:

- **I have a family to support.** My startups (Kindora, Outdoorithm) are real but don't generate meaningful income yet. I need steady revenue.
- **I love building.** I'm an engineer by training. Building is where my energy comes from.
- **The inbound doesn't match my offering.** Most people reaching out want fundraising help. That's not where I add unique value.

If I had unlimited runway, I'd focus solely on Kindora and Outdoorithm. This studio work is a bridge—a way to fund the journey while building skills that feed the startups.

**The north star remains:** The best outcome isn't that studio work scales. The best outcome is that Kindora or Outdoorithm takes off and I stop taking client projects entirely.

---

## 1.3 What I'm Explicitly Not Doing

**I'm not building a traditional dev shop.** I won't hire a bench of developers or chase volume. The moment I'm managing employees and selling contracts instead of building, the energy dies.

**I'm not doing legacy system overhauls.** I won't take projects requiring deep dives into existing codebases. My sweet spot is greenfield, AI-native builds—new applications from scratch.

**I'm not becoming a fundraising consultant.** Despite significant inbound interest, I'm stepping away from projects where the primary ask is "help us raise money."

**I'm not abandoning my startups.** Every client project should be evaluated against: does this get me closer to or further from the day I don't need client work?

# PART II: MARKET LANDSCAPE

## 2.1 The Four Market Archetypes

The mission-driven AI space contains four distinct archetypes:

### Archetype A: AI Training + Readiness (Dominant, Crowded)
- **Examples:** AI Consultants for Nonprofits, Nonprofit Tech for Good, Vertical Consulting
- **What they sell:** Confidence, literacy, governance, staff enablement
- **What they don't sell:** Production systems
- **Typical pricing:** $400-$4,000 for workshops/assessments
- **Takeaway:** Most "mission-driven AI" peers are selling confidence and governance, not production systems.

### Archetype B: Mission-Driven AI Consulting (Strategy + Prototype-to-Pilot)
- **Examples:** Avanzare Labs, Tangible AI, AI Force, Daly Analytics
- **What they sell:** Strategy, ethics, prototype development
- **What's missing:** Proof of repeated end-to-end production shipping
- **Typical pricing:** $100-$149/hr, $10k+ minimum projects
- **Takeaway:** Closest philosophically, but most present as "consultancy that can build," not "founder-led studio shipping production tools end-to-end."

### Archetype C: Funder/Intermediary Studio (Portfolio Enablement)
- **Examples:** American Journalism Project's AI Studio, Omdena
- **What they sell:** Field-building, grants, coaching, shared learning
- **Model:** Philanthropically funded, not fee-for-service
- **Takeaway:** This validates the funder wedge—they're already underwriting AI experimentation. My offer is the paid, high-velocity execution version.

### Archetype D: Solo/Micro-Studio, Fixed-Fee, Ship Fast
- **Examples:** Sugar (€9,800/2 weeks), Brandon Todd Jackson ($10k/4 weeks)
- **What they sell:** Speed, productized delivery
- **What's missing:** Domain expertise, mission alignment
- **Takeaway:** Productized, time-boxed builds are normalizing—but these are commodity-priced and domain-light. My advantage is charging premium because I bring domain authority + executive judgment + real shipping ability.

# PART IV: THE IP MODEL

## 4.1 The Wealth-Building Opportunity

Services businesses are hard to build wealth from—unless you're strategic about intellectual property.

Every project creates reusable patterns, components, and frameworks. Authentication systems, admin panels, matching algorithms, analytics dashboards—these compound over time.

**Principle:** I won't give away the factory every time I sell a car.

---

## 4.2 Three IP Options (Client Choice)

### Option A: Client-Owned (Premium)
- Client owns all foreground IP (what's unique to them)
- I retain background IP (my frameworks, libraries, templates)
- **20% price premium** for exclusivity
- Appropriate when: Client has regulatory or competitive reasons for ownership

### Option B: Licensed (Default)
- Client gets perpetual license to use the product
- I retain the right to reuse patterns, components, and learnings
- **Standard pricing**
- Appropriate when: Most projects

### Option C: Open Source (Funder-Friendly)
- Code is open-sourced under permissive license
- Appropriate when: Philanthropy is funding the build
- Aligns with funder expectations (navigated this at Google.org)

**Market validation:** This "no vendor lock-in, your IP" posture aligns with how modern AI builders talk. A.Team uses this exact language.

## 5.3 Partner Vetting Protocol

Before recommending any partner to a client, I validate their "AI-native" capability:

**The Test:**
1. Give them a small PR-sized task in my stack (auth edge case + UI tweak + test)
2. Ask them to record a 10-minute Loom explaining:
   - How they used AI tools (Cursor/Copilot/Claude Code)
   - What they verified manually
   - What risks they looked for

**The Standard:** If they can't explain their workflow clearly, they're not the right maintenance partner. Maintenance is mostly judgment.

**My Commitment:** I won't hand a client to a partner I haven't personally vetted through this process.

## Appendix D: The Decision to Not Scale

If the studio work takes off, I'll face a choice: stay a solo builder (3-4 projects/year) or build a small team.

**Arguments for staying solo:**
- Higher margin per project
- More flexibility
- No management overhead
- Protects time for startups

**Arguments for scaling:**
- More revenue potential
- Ability to take larger projects
- Build something beyond myself

**Current decision:** Stay solo through 2026. Revisit in 2027 based on Kindora/Outdoorithm trajectory and studio demand.

**The north star:** The best outcome isn't that studio work scales. The best outcome is that Kindora or Outdoorithm takes off and I stop taking client projects entirely.
