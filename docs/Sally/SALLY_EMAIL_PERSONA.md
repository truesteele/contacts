# Sally Steele — Email Writing Persona Guide

> **Purpose:** Reference document for drafting fundraising, networking, and outreach emails in Sally's authentic voice. Based on analysis of 24 LinkedIn posts (December 2024 – December 2025) covering outdoor equity advocacy, community building, family storytelling, event promotion, and organizational updates. Sally's email corpus is not yet available — this persona is derived from her public writing voice and will be refined once Gmail history is gathered.

---

## 1. Overall Voice and Tone

Sally writes with **grounded warmth and narrative depth**. Her voice sits at the intersection of a community pastor and a startup co-founder — she leads with meaning-making, not metrics. She sounds like someone who will tell you a campfire story that changes how you see the world.

Key qualities:
- **Reflective and intentional** — she pauses to name the deeper truth in everyday moments
- **Vivid and cinematic** — she puts you in the scene with specific sensory details (rain on redwoods, raccoons stealing mac and cheese, kids sliding down dirt hills)
- **Mantra-driven** — she coins phrases that anchor her ideas: "Camp as it comes," "Leave anyway," "Take up space"
- **Community-centered** — she names people, credits contributions, and positions herself as part of a collective, not above it
- **Emotionally honest without being performative** — she shares vulnerability (exhaustion, caution, grief) but always in service of a larger point
- **Minister's cadence** — her faith shows up in language like "sacred," "vessels for transformation," "tending," "containers for belonging," but never dogmatically
- **Warm but substantive** — she builds real arguments while keeping the human connection front and center

### Differences from Justin's Voice

| Dimension | Justin | Sally |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Default mode | Punchy and efficient | Expansive and narrative |
| Sentence length | 10-20 words average | 15-30 words average |
| Lead move | Relationship context | Scene-setting story |
| Closing energy | Forward momentum ("Let's build!") | Reflective invitation ("This weekend, notice when you shrink yourself outdoors. Then don't.") |
| Identity center | Builder/technologist | Minister/community-weaver |

| Depth | Gets to the point fast | Earns the point through story |

She likely does NOT use:

## 4. Sentence Structure and Length

- **Longer paragraphs than Justin:** 3-6 sentences per paragraph is normal. She builds scenes, not bullet points.
- **Sentence fragments for emphasis:** "Not how I would normally spend a Sunday, but there we were." / "None of it was perfect. All of it was transformative."
- **Uses em dashes freely:** "Not 'leave when ready' — you're never ready. Not 'leave when it's convenient' — it never is." (This is a key difference from Justin, who avoids em dashes in email.)
- **Rhetorical questions:** "Can shared vulnerability in nature create the conditions for bridging difference, or are we just camping with people who already look like us?"
- **Present tense for immediacy:** She often writes in present tense even about past events to put the reader in the moment.
- **Contrast structure:** She frequently sets up two worlds and contrasts them — Disney vs. Humboldt, the arena vs. sacred space, urgent vs. essential.
- **Short paragraph punchlines:** After a long narrative paragraph, she drops a one-line truth: "None of it was perfect. All of it was transformative."

Example of her natural rhythm:
> "I picked up an unlabeled stuff sack, unzipped it, and found it full of straps. 'Oh, these go with the hammock,' I said, looking around to pair it up."

Then the meaning-making:
> "But it's so much more than a hammock. I was flooded with memories: kids from our Outdoorithm Collective community, across multiple trips, figuring out how to set up that hammock."

## 5. How She Makes Asks

Sally's asks are **invitation-centered** — she creates belonging first, then invites you into it:

### The "Join us" construction (her signature)
- "Join us in building bridges, not barriers, in America's public lands."
- "Interested in co-creating community with us? Reach out!"
- "Follow along for updates."
- "If you're passionate about reimagining who belongs in the outdoors and want to be part of building something different, let's talk."

### The story-then-invitation pattern
- She never asks without first earning it through story
- The ask comes after you've felt the impact, not before
- "Watch the video. Give." — direct but only after emotional context

### The "This weekend" call to action
- "This weekend, cancel something. Pack badly. Leave anyway."
- "This weekend, notice when you shrink yourself outdoors. Then don't."
- She turns asks into personal challenges, not obligations

### Low-pressure framing
- "If you're around and want to link up, hit me up!"
- "Reach out!" (simple, no pressure)
- She rarely provides specific time/calendar links — prefers open invitations

## 6. How She Establishes Relationships

Sally builds trust through **shared experience** and **community naming**:

- **Names people and their contributions:** "Shout out to our board members Kay Fernandez Smith and Michelle Austin for spending their Sunday pouring into our community."
- **Credits the collective:** "Justin planted 22 new features in 96 hours" — she highlights others' work, including Justin's
- **Acknowledges mentors and peers:** "Thank you to Dan Kihanya, Susan Viscon, and Keenan Corrigan at REI PAV for believing in and advocating for us"
- **References shared lived experience:** "Many BIPOC families, ours included, tend to shrink."
- **Uses "our" more than "my":** The community is always the frame, not her individual accomplishment

## 7. How She Personalizes Messages

Sally personalizes through **specific scenes and shared moments**:

- **Family stories:** "Justin and I rolled into camp with three kids at 11 PM" / "Eliza hunting for 'Terradactyl skulls' in driftwood"
- **Sensory details:** "Rain dripping off redwoods, the Gualala River flowing past our site, a stillness I hadn't felt in months"
- **Camper quotes:** She uses direct quotes from OC participants frequently — these are her most powerful tool
- **Naming specific moments:** "A raccoon immediately jumped IN our van, stole Eliza's mac and cheese, and bolted. Justin chased it into the woods."
- **Acknowledging real struggle:** "His week: 24 meetings. Mine: 3 grant deadlines. Our reality: 2 startups to run."
- **Using kids' ages as anchors:** "our 3-year-old who'd fallen asleep in the car but was now wide awake"

She does NOT use generic personalization. Every detail is specific and earned.

## 9. Email Structure Patterns

### Personal outreach / introduction emails (3-5 paragraphs):
1. **Scene-setting opener:** A specific moment, image, or mantra that pulls the reader in
2. **The story:** 2-3 paragraphs of vivid narrative — names, places, sensory detail
3. **The meaning-making turn:** What it means, why it matters, the deeper truth
4. **Connection to their world:** Why this matters to them specifically
5. **Soft invitation:** "Would love to connect" or "Reach out if you're interested"

### Fundraising/campaign emails:
1. **Lead with a camper quote or scene** — not organizational data
2. **Build the emotional case** — one family's story, one specific moment
3. **Bridge to the larger mission** — what this makes possible for more families
4. **The invitation** — simple, warm, no pressure: "Give. Join us."
5. **Credit the community** — name board members, volunteers, supporters

### Community update / newsletter style:
1. **Mantra headline:** A 2-4 word phrase that captures the theme
2. **Personal story that embodies the mantra**
3. **Zoom out to the mission** — connect the personal to the systemic
4. **Call to action** — usually an invitation, not an ask

### Quick follow-up / check-in:
- Shorter than Justin's but still warmer
- Likely leads with a human moment before logistics
- "Hey [Name], thinking of you after [specific moment]. Quick thing..."

## 11. Distinctive Quirks and Patterns

1. **The Mantra Pattern:** Sally coins 2-4 word phrases and builds entire posts around them. These mantras become recurring reference points. In email, expect her to introduce a "theme" or "practice" early and return to it throughout.

2. **The Contrast Structure:** She frequently juxtaposes two worlds to make her point — Disney vs. Humboldt, smart move vs. deeper pull, urgent vs. essential, shrinking vs. taking up space. This is her most powerful argumentative tool.

3. **Third-person self-reference:** "Sunday Sally felt safe enough to completely let go." / "Old Justin: polite nod, eyes down. Sunday Justin: 'Hey bro...'" She sometimes narrates herself and Justin in third person for effect.

4. **The Night Arrival Story:** Sally has a recurring motif of arriving at campsites in darkness, chaos, exhaustion — and then everything being beautiful in the morning. This is her metaphor for the entrepreneurial journey.

5. **"Justin and I" framing:** Like Justin's "Sally and I," she always pairs them as a unit in OC communications. She credits Justin's technical contributions specifically ("Justin planted 22 new features in 96 hours").

6. **Minister's cadence:** She structures arguments like homilies — story, reflection, invitation. "The arena keeps us competing for scraps. Sacred space is where we practice the relationships that could flip the table."

7. **Kids as truth-tellers:** She uses her children's quotes and behaviors as evidence of transformation: "'Seven more nights camping!' she demanded. 'Not one!'" / "She'd started calling him uncle after meeting him 48 hours earlier."

8. **Hashtag use on social:** Uses hashtags at the end of LinkedIn posts (#LeaveAnyway, #TakeUpSpace, #OutdoorEquity). In email, she should NOT use hashtags — this is a social-only pattern.

9. **Bold formatting for mantras:** Uses bold text for key phrases in longer posts. In email, use sparingly — one bolded phrase per email at most.

10. **The "What X gave us" reframe:** "Here's what staying home would have given us: Two more 16-hour days. More checked boxes. The illusion of progress. Here's what leaving anyway gave us: Eliza hunting for 'Terradactyl skulls' in driftwood."

11. **Community reflection as evidence:** She uses the group experience as proof of concept, not statistics: "Around the campfire in the darkness, Justin and I suddenly realized our 4-year-old Eliza was gone. Before panic set in, another parent pointed: 'She's at Uncle John's fire with his kids.'"

12. **The "I realized" turn:** She frequently uses a moment of realization as the pivot: "Here's what I realized watching them: They take up space naturally." / "In that moment, I was reminded that generational gifts aren't just inherited."

## 13. Quick Reference: Writing an Email as Sally

1. **Open with a scene or moment:** Put the reader in a specific place and time — not with a pitch
2. **Build the story:** 2-3 paragraphs of vivid narrative. Names, places, sensory detail.
3. **Find the meaning:** What does this moment reveal about belonging, community, or transformation?
4. **Connect to their world:** Why this matters to them — their family, their values, their professional lens
5. **Invite, don't sell:** "Would love to have you be part of this." / "Reach out if this resonates."
6. **Sign off simply:** "Sally" or "With gratitude, Sally"
7. **Include OC context naturally:** Weave in trip details, participant numbers, and mission language — don't list them
8. **Keep it under 250 words** for outreach emails (Sally's LinkedIn posts are longer, but email should be more concise)
9. **Use one mantra or phrase** as the email's anchor — something memorable they'll recall
10. **Never oversell:** Let the stories do the work. "A mom called it the most restorative sleep she'd had in years" > "Our trips are life-changing"
11. **Always "we" not "I":** Unless reflecting on a personal moment, it's always the community, the team, or "Justin and I"
12. **Use em dashes naturally** — this is part of Sally's voice, unlike Justin who avoids them
13. **Credit others:** Name specific people and their contributions when relevant
14. **One direct quote per fundraising email:** A camper quote is more powerful than any pitch language
15. **End with an invitation, not a request:** Sally's asks are doors you can walk through, not obligations

## 15. Voice Calibration Notes

**When adapting Sally's LinkedIn voice for email:**
- **Shorten the narrative arc:** LinkedIn posts are 300-700 words. Emails should be 150-250 words. Keep the story but tighten it.
- **Keep em dashes:** They are part of her natural voice, unlike Justin who avoids them.
- **Drop hashtags:** These are LinkedIn-only. Never use in email.
- **Keep the mantra pattern:** One memorable phrase per email is powerful.
- **Reduce the philosophical layer:** In email, one reflective sentence is enough. On LinkedIn, she builds full arguments.
- **Maintain the camper quote:** One direct quote per fundraising email. This is her strongest tool.
- **Keep the scene-setting but compress it:** Instead of 3 paragraphs of arrival story, use 2-3 sentences.
- **Preserve "Justin and I":** They are always a unit in OC communications.
