### Post #8 | May 27-28, 2025
**Title:** Camp As It Comes - Memorial Day Trip to Pinnacles
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7333315147639205888
**Media:** 📷 Photos attached

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Impressions | 1,274 |

**Full Content:**

"Camp as it comes."

That's the new mantra we introduced this past weekend at Outdoorithm Collective's Memorial Day trip to Pinnacles National Park—and, as it turns out, we really did need it.

After squeezing in our 16-year-old's birthday celebration Friday night, Justin and I rolled into camp with three kids at 11 PM—after camp quiet hours, pitch black, three group sites to navigate, and a 3-year-old who'd fallen asleep in the car but was now wide awake and letting everyone know. We unhitched our trailer at the wrong site, dragged it across the street in the dark, and pounded tent stakes by headlamp.

Exhausted, we collapsed into our sleeping bags at midnight. Then at 1:30 AM, a tiny toddler voice: "Daddy, I threw up."

What followed was a middle-of-the-night cleanup involving eight compromised stuffies, a ruined sleeping bag, raccoons interested in our throw up trash, and two more rounds of illness before sunrise. Not exactly the peaceful camping arrival we'd envisioned as we prepared to welcome 40 novice campers to the site, many of them for their first camping trip.

But here's the thing—we kept laughing at ourselves and returning to our mantra: camp as it comes.

The next three days were magic. Forty folks from urban families came together at one of California's most dramatic national parks. A dozen kids under 10 formed an unbreakable pack, sliding down dirt hills, climbing ancient volcanic boulders, and exploring hidden caves. Teenagers mentored littles. Families from different backgrounds shared meals, stories, and genuine connection under star-filled skies.

None of it was perfect. All of it was transformative.

"Camp as it comes" isn't about lowering expectations—it's about raising our capacity for wonder, adaptation, and authentic community. It's recognizing that the most profound experiences happen not in controlled conditions, but when we learn to thrive with whatever we're given.

As we transition back to busy city life, we're holding onto this lesson: Whether it's 3 AM tent cleanup or awe-inspiring sunrise over volcanic peaks, it's all camping. It's all part of our story.

Through Outdoorithm Collective, we're creating these kinds of transformative outdoor experiences for urban families—especially those historically excluded from outdoor spaces. Because everyone deserves to discover that they belong in nature, exactly as they are.

Sometimes the best adventures are the ones that don't go according to plan.

#CampAsItComes #OutdoorEquity #CommunityBuilding #Nature #Resilience

**Title:** High Standards, Open Doors - Disney vs Humboldt
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7367194388990472194

**Full Content:**

**High Standards, Open Doors**

$11,300 at Disney. $100 at Humboldt Redwoods. One sorted us by income. The other showed us what America could be.

Yesterday's NYT piece ("Disney and the Decline of America's Middle Class") brought it all back. Our 2023 Disney trip was magical—character meals, light shows, riding Splash Mountain on its final day. First time there, loved it, but couldn't ignore how different our experience was from others. One sign indicated a 240 minute wait in line, we skipped to the front in 15 with our Lightning Passes. Every ride, no lines, breaks at our Grand Californian Hotel suite.

That was when Justin had his tech job. Google eliminated it last year. Now we are building Outdoorithm Collective, betting everything on bringing families together in nature.

Different story this July: Humboldt Redwoods with Outdoorithm Collective. 48 folks, 12 families. Our costs for campsite fees came out to about $25 per night for each family.

When a campervan got stuck on the river bank, the engineer, doctor, social worker, actor - everyone grabbed shovels and pushed. When it lurched free, we flexed like we'd won the championship.

Around the campfire in the darkness, Justin and I suddenly realized our 4-year-old Eliza was gone. Before panic set in, another parent pointed: "She's at Uncle John's fire with his kids." She'd started calling him uncle after meeting him 48 hours earlier. Now he was family.

The doctor taught the children to walk a rope line. A high schooler became bike instructor for the little ones. An educator showed them how to make glow stick glasses. Deep in the redwood groves, a survivor found healing in conversation with a street preacher - both giving each other life.

Our kids turned the cul-de-sac into a massive chalk mural, racing bikes through their art while we cooked communal dinners. Everyone flipped pancakes. Everyone washed dishes. Everyone's kids became everyone's responsibility.

At Disney, I knew exactly who could afford Lightning Passes. The algorithm sorted us perfectly.

At Humboldt, nobody sorted us at all. The wilderness doesn't check LinkedIn profiles. The river doesn't care about your ZIP code. For $25 a night, every family can experience the same ancient redwoods and the same swimming hole.

At Disney, setting high standards came with velvet ropes and price tags. At Humboldt, high standards meant everyone taking turns at the camp stove, everyone pushing that stuck van, everyone watching everyone's kids.

449 nights at Humboldt for the price of three at Disney.

We'll remember both trips. One for the magic money could buy. The other for the magic money couldn't touch.

The wilderness doesn't have Lightning Lanes. Let's keep it that way.

Join us in building bridges, not barriers, in America's public lands.

#OutdoorithmCollective #EveryoneBelongsOutdoors #PublicLands

I felt the gap before I could name it. Three days. Two rooms. Same crisis, different lenses.

In Philly at the Justice Outside conference last week, I stood by the elevators comparing notes with Daniel Hunter - organizer, movement trainer, author of Climate Resistance Handbook. In his keynote, he'd shared research showing it takes only 3.5% of a population acting collectively to create major political change.

The room felt like that 3.5% at work. Round tables. Practitioners from outdoor programs, advocacy groups, and think tanks imagining and building toward a new future—where urban kids claim redwoods as theirs, where families learn they belong in nature, where democracy gets rehearsed around campfires.

Three days later in New York City at Climate Week, the opening fireside chat at New Climate Futures focused on Team, TAM, and Tech. Compete with China. Win. Revenue. Someone noted that the word "climate" was barely being used -at Climate Week. The word "community" never came up at all.

I felt a little grief for what gets lost when we optimize for everything except people. And yet these are people building innovations we need: clean water systems, renewable energy, technologies that keep our planet from sliding further.

The two rooms weren't talking to each other.

Both are necessary. One builds community power. One builds literal power grids. But siloed, something essential gets lost.

Maybe the work isn't picking a room. Maybe it's recognizing they both matter.

When the goal is generating revenue and coming out on top, community work gets called a 'nice to have.' But it's so much more than a 'nice to have' - it's the meaning-making that helps people discover what's worth protecting.

People don't protect what they don't love. And love grows from belonging.

**Full Content:**

Leave anyway.

That's become a mantra at Outdoorithm Collective. Not "leave when ready"—you're never ready. Not "leave when it's convenient"—it never is. Just: Leave anyway.

Friday morning early, 1am. Justin and I collapsed into bed. We had 18 hours before we were supposed to check in for a campsite on the coast we'd booked blind six months ago.

His week: 24 meetings. Mine: 3 grant deadlines. Our reality: 2 startups to run.

The forecast: Atmospheric river hitting the west coast.

The smart move: Cancel everything. The deeper pull: Leave anyway.

Friday 5pm, Justin still closing client work. Me closing up grant applications racing to grab Eliza from school. We threw gear in our van like we were fleeing disaster—which we were. The disaster of letting urgent crowd out essential.

10pm arrival at Gualala Point. Pitch black. Justin checked our trailer. A raccoon immediately jumped IN our van, stole Eliza's mac and cheese, and bolted. Justin chased it into the woods, wrestled the container back.

Then the rain. All night. Hard.

But morning: rain dripping off redwoods, the Gualala River flowing past our site, a stillness I hadn't felt in months. This is what happens when you leave anyway.

At the beach, massive waves pounded while Eliza scrambled atop a driftwood castle others had built. "Seven more nights camping!" she demanded. "Not one!"

The trail filled despite the downpour—kids in head-to-toe rain gear, families who'd all left anyway.

Four families on that trail. One looked like ours. That's not a nature problem. That's an access problem.

This is why Outdoorithm Collective exists. Not everyone inherits camping knowledge. Not everyone has gear passed down through generations. But every family deserves to hear rain on redwoods, to feel their nervous system finally exhale.

Here's what staying home would have given us: Two more 16-hour days. More checked boxes. The illusion of progress.

Here's what leaving anyway gave us: Eliza hunting for "Terradactyl skulls" in driftwood. That 3am moment when rain on the van reminded us that some rhythms are older than deadlines.

The sacred pause doesn't come to you. You have to break the gravitational pull of your beautiful, overwhelming life.

*Leave anyway.* The hardest part isn't camping—it's leaving.

Nature doesn't check your calendar. It just waits.

This weekend, cancel something. Pack badly. Leave anyway.

#LeaveAnyway #SacredPause

### Post #18 | November 10, 2025
**Title:** Take Up Space - Sugarloaf Ridge State Park
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7393648716764729344

**Full Content:**

**Take up space.**

That's the newest mantra at Outdoorithm Collective. Not "be respectful" - that's always true. Not "know your place" - these places are yours too. Just: Take up space.

7am Sunday morning, Sugarloaf Ridge State Park. Justin Steele and I jolted awake to voices echoing across the campground:

"IT'S GONNA BLOW!!"

"Dad! Dad, I got one on!"

A family two sites down - boys fishing in the creek, yelling back to their campsite at dawn. My first thought? Honestly, I was kind of annoyed. My second: remembering how amazing it is to share firsts with your kids.

Later, their generator ran for thirty minutes. I noticed their trailer - duct tape holding panels together, missing side mirror, clearly making outdoor spaces work with what they had. Was the loudest generator in the world annoying? Yes. But they were taking up space with what they had.

Here's what I realized watching them: They take up space naturally. Loudly. Without checking if they're allowed.

And here's what I realized on the trail up Bald Mountain: we could, too. And we did - for the first time in three visits to this park.

Three guys coming down the fire road, one hauling a massive orange Gatorade cooler. Old Justin: polite nod, eyes down, keep moving. Sunday Justin: "Hey bro, making sure you don't run out of water?"

They burst out laughing. "We ran out anyway!"

I grinned at him, noticing the change.

At the summit - 360 degrees of the Bay Area sprawling below - I set my backpack down on a massive redwood bench and fell asleep. Never have I ever. But those three hours of sleep and the long hike caught up with me. Twenty minutes, out cold, while monarch butterflies circled. Sunday Sally felt safe enough to completely let go.

Small moments. Huge shift.

That's what taking up space looks like.

Families with generational camping rhythms don't wonder if they're being too loud, too present, too much. They fish at dawn because that's what their parents did. They run generators because they've always belonged here. They nap at summits without second-guessing.

Many BIPOC families, ours included, tend to shrink. We monitor our volume. We give deferential nods. We don't want to be tagged (or targeted) as not belonging - even though these lands are equally ours.

This is why Outdoorithm Collective exists. To give families permission to be fully present in outdoor spaces. To engage strangers on trails. To sleep at summits because you're tired and this mountain is yours, too.

Taking up space doesn't mean being inconsiderate. It means existing fully. Not shrinking yourself to make others comfortable in spaces that belong to everyone.

This weekend, notice when you shrink yourself outdoors. Then don't. Say the thing to the stranger on the trail. Stay longer at the viewpoint. Laugh louder around the campfire.

Take up space. These lands are yours too.

#TakeUpSpace #OutdoorEquity #PublicLands #Outdoorithm #Belonging
